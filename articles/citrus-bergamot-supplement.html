Citrus Bergamot: The Polyphenol That Rivals Statins for Cholesterol (2026 Guide)

By Sarah Mitchell, MS, RDN · May 2026 · 14 min read

Most people reach for red yeast rice or berberine when looking for a natural approach to cholesterol management. But citrus bergamot — an Italian citrus fruit grown almost exclusively in the Calabria region — has quietly accumulated some of the most impressive cardiovascular clinical data of any supplement available. Multiple randomized controlled trials show that bergamot polyphenol fraction (BPF) lowers LDL cholesterol by 25–40%, raises HDL by 20–40%, and reduces triglycerides — results comparable to low-dose statins, without the myopathy risk.

It's also the only natural compound with evidence for reducing small, dense LDL particles — the most atherogenic form of cholesterol — which most cholesterol supplements don't address at all. This guide covers the science, who should consider it, how to dose it, and the best bergamot supplements available in 2026.

What Is Citrus Bergamot?

Bergamot (Citrus bergamia) is a fragrant citrus fruit grown almost entirely in a narrow coastal strip of Calabria, in southern Italy. It's the ingredient that gives Earl Grey tea its distinctive flavor, and bergamot essential oil has been used in perfumery for centuries. But it's the fruit's juice — extraordinarily rich in polyphenols — that has become the focus of cardiovascular research.

Bergamot juice contains a unique profile of flavonoids not found at comparable concentrations in other citrus fruits:

  • Naringin — A flavanone glycoside with LDL-lowering and anti-inflammatory activity
  • Neohesperidin — Inhibits LDL oxidation and has vasodilatory effects
  • Bruteridin and melitidin — Bergamot-specific flavonoids that inhibit HMG-CoA reductase, the same enzyme targeted by statin drugs
  • Neoeriocitrin — Potent antioxidant activity, protective against oxidized LDL

The Bergamot Polyphenolic Fraction (BPF) — a standardized extract concentrating these compounds — is what's used in clinical research and quality supplements. Not all bergamot products on the market use a standardized BPF extract, which is why product selection matters significantly.

What the Clinical Research Shows

Lipid Panel Effects

The most comprehensive clinical trial of bergamot BPF was a randomized controlled trial by Mollace et al. (2011) published in Fitoterapia, involving 237 patients with hypercholesterolemia. Results after 30 days of 1,000mg BPF daily:

  • Total cholesterol reduced by 23%
  • LDL reduced by 24%
  • HDL increased by 22%
  • Triglycerides reduced by 30%
  • Blood glucose reduced by 19% in the metabolic syndrome subgroup

A second arm of the same study compared bergamot to rosuvastatin. Bergamot alone produced LDL reductions comparable to rosuvastatin 20mg. The combination of bergamot + rosuvastatin was superior to either alone, suggesting synergy with statin therapy — a finding with significant clinical implications for people who want to reduce their statin dose.

Small Dense LDL: The Key Differentiator

A 2019 study by Perna et al. found that bergamot extract significantly reduced small, dense LDL (sdLDL) particle concentration — the subtype most strongly associated with cardiovascular events. Standard lipid panels don't measure sdLDL specifically, but this particle type is more prone to arterial wall penetration and oxidation than large, buoyant LDL. No other widely available natural supplement has demonstrated consistent sdLDL reduction in randomized trials.

Endothelial Function and Cardiovascular Markers

Beyond lipids, bergamot polyphenols have demonstrated improvements in arterial stiffness (measured by pulse wave velocity), C-reactive protein (CRP — a marker of vascular inflammation), and endothelial nitric oxide production. A 2020 randomized trial in patients with metabolic syndrome found significant improvements in vascular endothelial function after 60 days of bergamot supplementation.

Blood Glucose Effects

Several trials have noted concurrent improvements in fasting glucose and insulin sensitivity in bergamot studies — particularly in subjects with metabolic syndrome or prediabetes. The proposed mechanism involves improved GLUT4 translocation in skeletal muscle and hepatic glucose metabolism. Bergamot is one of the few cardiovascular-targeted supplements with meaningful secondary evidence for metabolic improvement.

Who Should Consider Citrus Bergamot?

  • People with mildly to moderately elevated LDL who want to try a natural approach before or alongside medication
  • Statin users who want to reduce their dose — bergamot + low-dose statin may equal full statin dose in LDL reduction
  • People with low HDL — bergamot consistently raises HDL, which most interventions don't
  • Those with metabolic syndrome — simultaneous benefit for glucose, triglycerides, and cholesterol
  • People with high triglycerides — consistent 25–40% reduction across trials
  • Anyone concerned about cardiovascular risk who prefers a food-based intervention

Note: If you have a confirmed cardiovascular diagnosis or are on lipid-lowering medications, discuss bergamot supplementation with your physician before starting.

Dosing

  • Standard dose: 500–1,500mg of standardized BPF extract per day
  • Trials using 500mg BPF: Meaningful but modest effects — best for maintenance
  • Trials using 1,000–1,500mg BPF: Largest LDL reduction and HDL increase
  • Timing: Take with meals — polyphenols absorb better with dietary fat
  • Duration: Most trials show significant effects at 30 days; maximal benefit at 60–90 days

Key label check: Look for products standardized to BPF (Bergamot Polyphenolic Fraction) at a specified percentage — typically 25–38%. Products listing only raw bergamot powder without standardization are unlikely to replicate clinical trial results.

Safety and Side Effects

Citrus bergamot has a favorable safety profile in clinical studies to date. Reported adverse effects are rare and mild — occasional GI discomfort at high doses. There is no evidence of the myopathy (muscle damage) associated with statins.

Drug interactions to be aware of:

  • Statins: Bergamot polyphenols may inhibit CYP3A4 enzymes (similar to grapefruit), potentially increasing statin blood levels. Discuss with your physician if combining with statins.
  • Blood glucose medications: The glucose-lowering effects of bergamot may add to antidiabetic drugs — monitor glucose levels if combining.
  • Anticoagulants: Theoretical interaction; discuss with your prescriber.

The Bottom Line

Citrus bergamot BPF is one of the most clinically validated natural supplements for cardiovascular health available. With multiple randomized controlled trials showing LDL reductions of 24–40% and HDL increases of 20–40%, it performs well beyond most "heart health" supplements. The unique evidence for reducing small, dense LDL particles — combined with triglyceride reduction and metabolic benefits — makes it a compelling first-line natural intervention for people with mild to moderate cholesterol concerns.

For most people, Jarrow Formulas Citrus Bergamot 500mg at 1,000mg/day (2 capsules) is the best-value starting point. Those wanting clinical-grade manufacturing should choose Thorne Bergamet Pro+. And if you're already on a statin, discuss whether adding bergamot could allow your physician to reduce your statin dose.
