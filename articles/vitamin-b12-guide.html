Vitamin B12 Guide: Deficiency, Forms, Dosage & Best Supplements

By the VitalGuide Editorial Team · April 2026 · 9 min read

Vitamin B12 (cobalamin) is one of the most critical vitamins in human biology — essential for neurological function, red blood cell formation, DNA synthesis, and energy metabolism. It's also one of the most commonly deficient nutrients in modern populations, with particularly high deficiency rates among vegans and vegetarians (B12 is found almost exclusively in animal products), older adults (who produce less intrinsic factor needed for absorption), and people taking Metformin (which blocks B12 absorption). Low B12 can present as fatigue, brain fog, numbness and tingling in extremities, mood changes, and in severe cases, irreversible neurological damage — making it a nutrient worth taking seriously.

The confusion around B12 supplementation often centers on which form to use. Cyanocobalamin is cheap and stable; methylcobalamin and adenosylcobalamin are the active, coenzyme forms the body actually uses. This guide cuts through the noise with clear, evidence-based guidance on testing, forms, dosing, and product selection.

Who Is at Risk for B12 Deficiency?

  • Vegans and vegetarians: B12 is found in meaningful quantities only in animal-sourced foods (meat, fish, dairy, eggs). Vegans who don't supplement are virtually guaranteed to develop deficiency over time — typically within 3–5 years of strict vegan diet.
  • Older adults (50+): Gastric acid production and intrinsic factor secretion decline with age, impairing the ability to extract B12 from food. The NIH recommends that adults over 50 get most of their B12 from supplements or fortified foods precisely because absorption from dietary sources becomes unreliable.
  • Metformin users: The diabetes medication Metformin directly reduces B12 absorption in the intestine. Studies show that 10–30% of long-term Metformin users develop B12 deficiency. B12 monitoring and supplementation are recommended for all Metformin patients.
  • Those with digestive conditions: Crohn's disease, celiac disease, atrophic gastritis, and previous gastric surgery all reduce B12 absorption. Pernicious anemia (autoimmune destruction of intrinsic factor-producing cells) causes severe malabsorption requiring injectable or sublingual B12.
  • Chronic alcohol users: Alcohol impairs B12 absorption and metabolism.

The Forms of B12: Which Is Best?

  • Methylcobalamin: The most biologically active form; the natural coenzyme form found in human tissues. Does not require conversion before use. Preferred by many functional medicine practitioners, particularly for neurological applications. May be retained in tissues longer than cyanocobalamin.
  • Adenosylcobalamin: The other active coenzyme form; most concentrated in mitochondria and important for energy metabolism. Often combined with methylcobalamin in "active B12" supplements.
  • Cyanocobalamin: The most common, most stable, and cheapest synthetic form. Must be converted to methylcobalamin or adenosylcobalamin before use — an efficient process in healthy individuals. Releases a tiny amount of cyanide during conversion, though at supplemental doses this is physiologically negligible and not a meaningful safety concern. Well-studied and FDA-approved. A reasonable choice for most healthy adults; may be suboptimal for those with MTHFR variants or impaired methylation.
  • Hydroxocobalamin: A naturally occurring intermediate form, often used in B12 injections. Retained in the body longer than cyanocobalamin and can serve as a cofactor in detoxification pathways. Sometimes preferred for high-dose deficiency treatment.

Dosing and Absorption Considerations

B12 has a unique two-mechanism absorption system: at low doses (under ~10mcg), absorption depends on intrinsic factor and is tightly regulated (~50% efficiency). At high doses (500–1,000mcg+), a small but meaningful percentage is absorbed passively through the gut lining, bypassing the intrinsic factor requirement entirely. This is why very high-dose oral B12 supplements (1,000–5,000mcg) can correct deficiency even in those with impaired intrinsic factor — enough passive absorption occurs to build up stores.

  • General maintenance (healthy adults with dietary intake): 100–500mcg/day
  • Vegans and vegetarians: 500–1,000mcg/day of cyanocobalamin, or 250–500mcg of methylcobalamin; alternatively, 2,500mcg 2–3 times per week
  • Deficiency correction: 1,000–2,000mcg/day for 1–3 months to replenish stores, then maintenance dosing
  • Older adults with absorption concerns: 1,000mcg/day sublingual (under-the-tongue dissolving) bypasses gastric intrinsic factor requirements more efficiently than swallowed tablets
  • Sublingual vs. oral: Sublingual B12 delivers some absorption directly through oral mucous membranes, partially bypassing gastric absorption entirely. This is the preferred delivery method for those with gastric absorption issues short of injections.

The Bottom Line

Vitamin B12 deficiency is a serious and underdiagnosed problem — but it's also one of the easiest to prevent and treat with the right supplementation. If you're vegan, vegetarian, over 50, taking Metformin, or have any digestive condition affecting absorption, B12 supplementation is not optional — it's essential. Choose methylcobalamin or the methyl+adenosyl combination for the most direct active form; use sublingual delivery for superior absorption; and dose aggressively (1,000–5,000mcg) if you have absorption concerns. Get a baseline serum B12 test to confirm your starting status and monitor your response to supplementation.
