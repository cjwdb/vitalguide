L-Citrulline: Why It's the Most Effective Natural Performance Supplement

By the VitalGuide Editorial Team · April 2026 · 10 min read

If you want better endurance, stronger muscle pumps, faster recovery between sets, and improved blood flow — L-citrulline is probably the single most evidence-backed supplement you can take for those goals. It's the compound that should be in your pre-workout. The problem is that most commercial pre-workouts contain it in inadequate doses, hiding behind proprietary blends to obscure that you're getting 1–2g when 6–8g is what the research actually uses.

Understanding why L-citrulline works — and how to dose it correctly — is the difference between a supplement that delivers real results and one that just gives you a color-labeled placebo. This guide covers the science, the forms, the doses, and the best standalone products.

What Is L-Citrulline and How Does It Work?

L-citrulline is a non-essential amino acid found naturally in watermelon (the name comes from Citrullus lanatus). Unlike essential amino acids that are incorporated into proteins, citrulline primarily functions as an intermediate in the urea cycle — the metabolic pathway that processes ammonia in the liver and kidneys.

The key performance mechanism is the nitric oxide (NO) pathway:

  1. L-citrulline is converted to L-arginine in the kidneys — more efficiently than supplementing arginine directly
  2. L-arginine serves as the substrate for nitric oxide synthase (NOS) enzymes
  3. NOS enzymes produce nitric oxide (NO) from arginine
  4. NO causes smooth muscle relaxation and vasodilation — widening of blood vessels
  5. Wider blood vessels mean more blood flow, more oxygen delivery, better nutrient uptake in working muscles, and improved removal of metabolic waste (lactate, ammonia)

The reason L-citrulline outperforms L-arginine supplements is pharmacokinetics. When you take L-arginine orally, it is rapidly degraded by arginase enzymes in the gut and liver before it reaches the bloodstream — a process called first-pass metabolism. L-citrulline bypasses this degradation, is absorbed intact, converted to arginine in the kidneys, and results in significantly higher plasma arginine levels than direct arginine supplementation. Multiple studies have confirmed this counterintuitive finding: citrulline is more effective at raising arginine levels than arginine itself.

Performance Benefits with Strong Evidence

Improved Muscular Endurance and Repetitions

The most replicated finding in L-citrulline research is increased muscular endurance. A 2010 study in the Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research found that 8g of citrulline malate taken 1 hour before lower-body training significantly increased the number of repetitions performed across all sets compared to placebo — with the effect most pronounced in later sets (when fatigue typically limits output). A 2015 meta-analysis of 12 trials confirmed that citrulline supplementation produced statistically significant improvements in both resistance training and aerobic endurance performance.

Reduced Muscle Soreness

The same 2010 study noted a 40% reduction in muscle soreness 24–48 hours post-training in the citrulline group compared to placebo. The proposed mechanism is improved ammonia clearance — ammonia accumulation during exercise contributes significantly to both acute fatigue and delayed muscle soreness. Because citrulline supports the urea cycle (which processes ammonia), higher citrulline levels mean faster clearance of this waste product.

Cardiovascular Performance and Blood Pressure

Beyond acute exercise performance, chronic L-citrulline supplementation has meaningful cardiovascular effects. A 2017 meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials found that citrulline supplementation significantly reduced both systolic and diastolic blood pressure, with the effect being most pronounced in individuals with elevated baseline blood pressure. The vasodilation mechanism that improves performance also has direct cardiovascular health benefits — improved arterial flexibility, reduced peripheral resistance, and enhanced endothelial function.

The Pump Effect

The "pump" — the sensation of muscles engorging with blood during training — is not merely cosmetic. It reflects real vasodilation and increased blood flow to working muscles, which enhances oxygen and nutrient delivery and supports performance. L-citrulline is one of the most effective pump agents available, and unlike some pump supplements that work primarily through osmotic mechanisms, citrulline's NO-mediated pump has functional performance benefits alongside the aesthetic effect.

L-Citrulline vs Citrulline Malate: Which Form Is Better?

You'll encounter two primary forms when shopping for citrulline:

  • Pure L-Citrulline: The amino acid itself, ~100% citrulline by weight
  • Citrulline Malate (2:1 ratio): Citrulline bound to malic acid in a 2:1 ratio by weight — so a 6g dose of citrulline malate delivers approximately 4g of citrulline plus 2g of malate

Malic acid (malate) is an intermediate in the Krebs cycle (the energy production pathway in mitochondria). The addition of malate was originally proposed to enhance ATP production and reduce fatigue through complementary mechanisms. Many of the landmark studies on citrulline for exercise performance used the malate form.

The current evidence does not clearly demonstrate that malate adds meaningfully beyond the citrulline component. However, because many studies used citrulline malate (often at 6–8g, delivering ~4–5.3g citrulline), there is more direct research data for the malate form in exercise contexts. Both forms are legitimate. If your goal is maximizing citrulline content per gram, pure L-citrulline is more efficient. If you prefer the form most directly studied, citrulline malate at 6–8g is well-supported.

Optimal Dosing

  • Pure L-Citrulline: 3–6g taken 30–60 minutes before exercise
  • Citrulline Malate (2:1): 6–8g taken 30–60 minutes before exercise (delivering ~4–5.3g citrulline)
  • For cardiovascular/blood pressure benefits: 3–6g daily, timing flexible

Most commercial pre-workouts contain 1–3g of citrulline — well below the 4–6g threshold that research consistently shows is needed for meaningful performance effects. This is why standalone citrulline supplementation typically outperforms generic pre-workouts for people who understand the research.

L-citrulline is very well tolerated at these doses. Gastrointestinal distress is uncommon (unlike arginine, which frequently causes GI issues at high doses). The main precaution is for individuals taking medications for erectile dysfunction (PDE5 inhibitors like Viagra) or blood pressure medications — the additive vasodilation could be clinically significant. Consult a healthcare provider if you are on either.

Stacking L-Citrulline

L-citrulline pairs well with several other evidence-backed sports nutrition ingredients:

  • Creatine monohydrate: Creatine improves power output and short-duration high-intensity performance; citrulline improves endurance and reduces fatigue. These are complementary rather than overlapping mechanisms.
  • Beta-alanine: Beta-alanine buffers acidic pH in muscle during high-intensity exercise. Used alongside citrulline, the combination addresses both blood flow (citrulline) and muscular fatigue (beta-alanine) — though beta-alanine requires loading and produces the harmless "tingling" sensation called paresthesia.
  • Electrolytes: Adequate sodium, potassium, and magnesium support the vasodilatory effects of citrulline and are essential for muscle contraction and hydration during training.

The Bottom Line

L-citrulline is one of the most evidence-backed performance supplements available — with replicated evidence for increased endurance, reduced muscle soreness, improved pump, and cardiovascular benefits. The key is using it at an effective dose (6g+ of citrulline malate or 4–5g+ of pure L-citrulline), which most commercial pre-workouts fail to deliver.
