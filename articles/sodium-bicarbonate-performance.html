Sodium Bicarbonate for Athletic Performance: Does Baking Soda Actually Work? (2026)

By the VitalGuide Editorial Team · April 2026 · 11 min read

Sodium bicarbonate — ordinary baking soda — is one of the most researched and evidence-backed ergogenic (performance-enhancing) aids in sports science. It's been studied extensively since the 1980s, is completely legal in competitive sport, and is available for essentially nothing. Yet most athletes have never tried it, and many have never heard of it.

The research is clear: for high-intensity exercise lasting 1–7 minutes, sodium bicarbonate consistently improves performance by 1–3% in well-controlled trials — an effect comparable to many expensive supplements. Here's how it works, who benefits, and exactly how to use it without gastrointestinal disaster.

How Sodium Bicarbonate Works

During intense exercise, your muscles produce hydrogen ions (H⁺) as a byproduct of anaerobic metabolism. As H⁺ concentration rises, muscle pH drops — the environment becomes more acidic — and this acidosis directly impairs muscle contraction. The burning sensation you feel during a hard set of squats or a 400-meter sprint is partly this acidosis at work.

Sodium bicarbonate is an alkaline compound — it raises blood pH. When you consume it before exercise, it acts as an extracellular buffer: it increases the concentration of bicarbonate ions in the blood, which can more readily accept the H⁺ being exported from working muscles. This delays the onset of acidosis, allowing muscles to continue contracting at high intensity for longer.

Think of it as expanding your body's acid-buffering capacity before you need it.

What the Research Shows

Performance in High-Intensity Exercise

A 2021 meta-analysis in Sports Medicine covering 87 studies found that sodium bicarbonate supplementation produced a mean improvement of 1.7% in time-to-exhaustion tests and meaningful performance gains in swimming, cycling, rowing, combat sports, and team sport sprints. Effect sizes were consistently largest for efforts lasting 1–7 minutes — the "glycolytic zone" where acidosis is most performance-limiting.

Strength and Power

Sodium bicarbonate also benefits strength athletes. A 2013 study found that 0.3 g/kg NaHCO₃ improved total repetitions in bench press to failure by ~5.5%. Similar benefits have been found for multiple sets with short rest periods, CrossFit-style workouts, and combat sport bouts where repeated explosive efforts are required.

Endurance Events

Benefits diminish for events primarily fueled by aerobic metabolism (distance running, marathon, long-distance cycling). The acidosis mechanism is less dominant when mitochondrial aerobic pathways are the primary energy source. However, the final sprint or climb at the end of an endurance event — where glycolytic intensity spikes — may still benefit.

Who Benefits Most from Sodium Bicarbonate?

  • Swimmers — 100m and 200m freestyle, butterfly, breaststroke; one of the most studied populations
  • Cyclists — Time trials, track cycling, criteriums with repeated hard efforts
  • Combat sports athletes — Boxing, wrestling, MMA (repeated high-intensity bursts)
  • Track and field — 400m, 800m, 1500m runners
  • Rowers — 2000m ergometer and on-water racing
  • Team sport athletes — Soccer, basketball, rugby (repeated sprint performance)
  • CrossFit and HIIT athletes — High-rep lifting, AMRAPs, circuit training

Dosing Protocol

Standard Dosing

The most evidence-backed protocol is 0.2–0.3 g/kg body weight taken 60–90 minutes before exercise. For a 75 kg (165 lb) athlete, this is 15–22.5 grams — roughly 1–1.5 tablespoons of baking soda. Blood bicarbonate peaks approximately 60–90 minutes after ingestion.

Low-Dose Option

For those sensitive to GI side effects (see below), some research supports a lower dose of 0.1–0.2 g/kg, which still provides meaningful but reduced buffering capacity with significantly better tolerability.

Multi-Day Loading

A 2018 study in the Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition demonstrated that splitting the dose across 3 days (0.5 g/kg/day divided into three doses of ~167 mg/kg per dose taken with meals) achieves comparable blood bicarbonate elevations with dramatically reduced GI distress. This multi-day approach is increasingly recommended for athletes competing in tournaments or back-to-back events.

Managing GI Side Effects

The primary limitation of sodium bicarbonate supplementation is gastrointestinal distress: nausea, bloating, diarrhea, and stomach cramping can occur, especially at higher doses. Strategies to minimize these effects:

  • Take with food — consuming NaHCO₃ alongside a carbohydrate-containing meal slows absorption and significantly reduces GI symptoms
  • Use the multi-day loading protocol — distributing the dose across days substantially improves tolerability
  • Start low and test — begin with 0.1 g/kg in training to gauge individual tolerance before competition
  • Use slow-release capsules — commercial sodium bicarbonate supplements in slow-release capsule form (like Bicarb System or similar) reduce peak GI exposure
  • Allow a 2+ hour gap before competition — most GI effects pass within 90–120 minutes of ingestion

Sodium Bicarbonate Supplements and Products

1. Arm & Hammer Pure Baking Soda — Cheapest Option

The simplest and cheapest option: pharmaceutical-grade sodium bicarbonate is identical to food-grade baking soda. A 5 lb bag of Arm & Hammer baking soda is essentially a lifetime supply for an athlete using it pre-workout and costs just a few dollars. Weigh your dose on a kitchen scale for accuracy. The only downside is the taste (salty, mildly alkaline) and GI intolerance if not managed properly.

Best for: Athletes on a budget who are comfortable with the raw powder approach and willing to experiment with timing and food co-ingestion.

2. Slow-Release Sodium Bicarbonate Capsules — Best for GI Tolerance

Check Price: Sodium Bicarbonate Capsules on Amazon

For athletes who experience significant GI distress with raw baking soda, encapsulated slow-release sodium bicarbonate products are a meaningful upgrade. These products use enteric or delayed-release coatings to slow absorption and smooth out peak bicarbonate concentration in the blood, reducing the acute GI load. While more expensive than raw baking soda, they can be the difference between a usable protocol and a race-day disaster.

Best for: Competitive athletes who need reliable GI tolerance on race day; those who've experienced significant side effects from raw baking soda.

3. Beta-Alanine (as a Stack Partner) — Complementary Buffer

Check Price: Beta-Alanine Powder on Amazon

Beta-alanine and sodium bicarbonate work via complementary mechanisms. Beta-alanine raises intramuscular carnosine (an intracellular buffer), while sodium bicarbonate elevates extracellular blood bicarbonate. Research shows combining them produces additive performance benefits greater than either alone — making them a natural pre-workout stack for high-intensity athletes. Beta-alanine requires chronic loading (2–5 g/day for 4+ weeks); sodium bicarbonate is taken acutely pre-performance.

Best for: Athletes seeking maximum buffering capacity from both intracellular and extracellular mechanisms.

The Bottom Line

Sodium bicarbonate is one of the few sports supplements with a genuinely robust evidence base and near-zero cost. If you compete or train at high intensities lasting 1–7 minutes, it is worth experimenting with. The main obstacle is GI tolerance — but with proper dosing strategy (food co-ingestion, slow-release capsules, or multi-day loading), most athletes can manage this effectively.

Start by testing it in training at 0.2 g/kg with a carbohydrate-rich meal 90 minutes before a hard session. If you tolerate it well, you have a safe, legal, evidence-backed performance tool that most of your competitors are ignoring.
