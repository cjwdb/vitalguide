Best Hydration Multipliers 2026: LMNT vs Liquid IV vs Nuun Compared

By the VitalGuide Editorial Team · May 2026 · 15 min read

Proper hydration is foundational to performance, recovery, cognitive function, and long-term health. But water alone isn't always enough. During exercise, illness, heat exposure, or following a low-carb or ketogenic diet, the body loses electrolytes — sodium, potassium, magnesium, chloride — that water simply cannot replace. Electrolyte depletion, even mild, drives fatigue, muscle cramps, brain fog, and decreased performance before you even feel thirsty.

The Science of Hydration: Beyond Plain Water

Hydration is not simply a function of how much water you drink. It is a function of fluid retention — and fluid retention depends critically on electrolyte balance, particularly sodium. Sodium is the primary extracellular cation and the dominant driver of osmotic pressure that determines whether fluid stays in your bloodstream and cells or passes through into the urine.

When you exercise, you lose sodium in sweat — not just water. Replacing the water without replacing the sodium creates a state called hyponatremia in severe cases, or sub-optimal hydration status in milder cases. You can drink a liter of plain water after a hard workout and still feel poorly hydrated because your sodium levels haven't been restored enough to maintain proper fluid distribution.

The World Health Organization's Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS) research established the foundational science: the combination of glucose and sodium in the correct ratio activates the sodium-glucose co-transporter (SGLT1) in the small intestine, dramatically accelerating water absorption into the bloodstream compared to water or glucose alone. This mechanism — called Cellular Transport Technology by Liquid IV — is the same principle underlying clinical ORS therapy used to treat severe dehydration from cholera and gastroenteritis worldwide.

The Sodium Debate: How Much Is Enough?

The sodium content in hydration products varies dramatically — from 200mg per serving (Nuun Sport) to 1000mg per serving (LMNT). This is where the most meaningful product differentiation occurs.

The right sodium dose depends on your context:

  • Intense exercise or heat: Sweat sodium concentration averages 900mg per liter of sweat, ranging from 200mg to 1,700mg depending on the individual. Heavy sweaters and endurance athletes may lose 2,000–4,000mg of sodium per hour. At this level, 1000mg per serving (LMNT) is physiologically appropriate.
  • Low-carb / ketogenic diet: Insulin suppression on a low-carb diet causes the kidneys to excrete more sodium. This "keto flu" is largely a sodium-depletion phenomenon. LMNT was originally designed for this population specifically, and 1000mg sodium per serving addresses the elevated need.
  • General daily hydration: For desk workers, casual exercisers, and general daily hydration, 200–500mg sodium per serving is adequate and better suited to avoid unnecessary sodium loading.
  • Illness and GI recovery: WHO ORS targets approximately 75mEq/L sodium (about 1750mg/L when dissolved in 1L water). DripDrop and Liquid IV approximate this ratio for rapid rehydration.

Sugar in Electrolyte Drinks: Friend or Foe?

Sugar in electrolyte drinks serves a specific physiological function: activating the SGLT1 co-transporter for rapid intestinal fluid absorption. In the context of WHO ORS and clinical rehydration, glucose is not just a flavoring agent — it's a transport mechanism. Liquid IV and DripDrop use this intentionally.

For most athletes and healthy adults, however, additional glucose during moderate exercise or daily hydration is unnecessary. Sugar-free formulas (LMNT, Ultima, Nuun) use stevia or other non-nutritive sweeteners and still provide full electrolyte replenishment without the glycemic impact. They're better suited to daily use, keto/low-carb dieters, and anyone watching caloric intake.

Bottom line: Sugar-containing formulas are superior for acute dehydration recovery from illness or intense exercise. Sugar-free is preferable for regular daily use and low-carb diets.

Who Actually Needs an Electrolyte Supplement?

Electrolyte supplements provide the most value for specific populations. If you don't fit these categories, plain water is likely sufficient:

  • Endurance athletes: Anyone sweating heavily for 60+ minutes needs sodium replacement, not just fluid replacement.
  • People on low-carb or ketogenic diets: Reduced insulin leads to increased urinary sodium excretion — supplementation directly addresses keto flu symptoms.
  • Hot climate residents or summer outdoor workers: Chronic heat exposure drives chronic sodium loss beyond what a typical diet replaces.
  • People recovering from illness: GI illness, vomiting, diarrhea, and fever all drive accelerated electrolyte depletion where ORS-type formulas provide direct benefit.
  • Frequent travelers: Air travel is dehydrating (cabin humidity is typically below 20%); alcohol at airports compounds the effect. A hydration packet is practical travel kit support.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is LMNT better than Liquid IV?

It depends on your use case. LMNT is better for athletes, keto dieters, and heavy sweaters because of its 1000mg sodium content and zero sugar. Liquid IV is better for acute dehydration recovery — illness, travel, or hangover — because its glucose-sodium formula accelerates fluid absorption via the WHO ORS mechanism. For daily hydration, neither is ideal — Ultima or Nuun are better suited to regular low-intensity use.

Can I drink electrolyte supplements every day?

Yes — daily electrolyte use is appropriate for athletes, people in hot climates, or those on low-carb diets. For sedentary individuals with normal diets, daily electrolyte supplementation is generally unnecessary but harmless at low-to-moderate sodium doses. People with hypertension or kidney disease should consult a physician before regular high-sodium electrolyte use (LMNT's 1000mg per serving, in particular).

Do electrolyte drinks help with hangovers?

Yes, with an important caveat. Alcohol inhibits the anti-diuretic hormone (ADH), causing the kidneys to excrete water and electrolytes — leading to the dehydration that contributes to hangover symptoms. Replenishing electrolytes alongside water the morning after (or before sleep) can meaningfully reduce headache, fatigue, and brain fog. Liquid IV or DripDrop ORS are best for this use given their glucose-assisted rapid absorption.

What electrolytes do I need for keto?

On a ketogenic diet, the key electrolytes are sodium (most critical), potassium, and magnesium. Reduced insulin levels cause the kidneys to excrete more sodium, which drives the "keto flu" — symptoms including fatigue, headaches, muscle cramps, and brain fog. LMNT was specifically designed for this population with 1000mg sodium, 200mg potassium, and 60mg magnesium per serving. Many keto practitioners also add supplemental magnesium glycinate separately for additional coverage.

How much water should I drink with an electrolyte packet?

Follow the product label guidance, but the general principle is: more water creates a more dilute (hypotonic) solution that is absorbed faster; less water creates a more concentrated (hypertonic) solution that absorbs more slowly. LMNT recommends 16–32oz (500mL–1L) per packet. Liquid IV recommends exactly 16oz. Nuun recommends 16oz per tablet. Mixing with too little water (particularly for Liquid IV's higher-sugar formula) can slow absorption and cause GI discomfort.
