🍓 Best Urolithin A Supplements 2026: Mitophagy, Muscle & Longevity

By the VitalGuide Editorial Team · April 2026 · 15 min read

Urolithin A is one of the most compelling longevity compounds to emerge from recent clinical research. Unlike many supplements that rely primarily on animal studies, Urolithin A has human clinical trial data showing improvements in muscle endurance, mitochondrial function, and cellular health markers — making it a genuinely exciting addition to the science-based longevity toolkit.

But here's what makes Urolithin A unusual: you can't simply eat it. It's produced by your gut bacteria when they metabolize ellagitannins — compounds found in pomegranates, walnuts, and certain berries. The problem is that only about 30–40% of the population has the right gut microbiome composition to produce meaningful amounts of Urolithin A from food. For everyone else, direct supplementation is the only way to get it.

This guide covers what Urolithin A is, what the science actually says, and which supplements deliver it most reliably.

What Is Urolithin A?

Urolithin A (UA) is a gut microbiome-derived metabolite. The pathway works like this: you eat foods containing ellagitannins (found in pomegranates, strawberries, raspberries, walnuts, and oak-aged wines) → your gut bacteria convert them to ellagic acid → certain bacteria (primarily Gordonibacter and Ellagibacter species) further metabolize ellagic acid into urolithins, including Urolithin A.

The resulting compound — Urolithin A — is a small, lipophilic molecule that crosses cell membranes easily and has been shown to accumulate in multiple tissues including muscle, fat, liver, and brain.

How Urolithin A Works: Mitophagy

Urolithin A's primary mechanism is mitophagy induction — the selective recycling of damaged mitochondria. This is a critical anti-aging process.

Here's why mitophagy matters: mitochondria are the energy-producing organelles in every cell. As we age, mitochondria accumulate damage — from oxidative stress, replication errors, and normal metabolic byproducts. Damaged mitochondria produce less ATP, generate more reactive oxygen species (ROS), and eventually trigger inflammatory signaling.

Mitophagy is the cell's quality-control mechanism for mitochondria: tagging damaged ones for degradation and replacement. This process declines with age, contributing to the gradual deterioration of mitochondrial function characteristic of aging muscle, brain, and other high-energy tissues.

Urolithin A activates mitophagy by:

  • Upregulating PINK1/Parkin — the key mitophagy signaling pathway
  • Inducing autophagy genes (LC3, Beclin-1) that facilitate mitochondrial recycling
  • Activating AMPK, which further promotes mitochondrial turnover
  • Inhibiting mTORC1, which otherwise suppresses autophagy

The net effect: clearer out damaged mitochondria, make room for new healthy ones, and restore mitochondrial respiratory capacity.

The Human Clinical Evidence

Most supplement categories are supported primarily by animal data. Urolithin A is unusual in having multiple high-quality human randomized controlled trials.

The Amazentis/Timeline Clinical Trials

Amazentis (the company behind the Mitopure® brand, now marketed as Timeline) has conducted the most rigorous Urolithin A human research:

  • 2019 (Nature Metabolism): First-in-human RCT showing Urolithin A is safe, well-absorbed, and dose-dependently activates mitophagy biomarkers (including cardiolipin remodeling) in skeletal muscle. This was landmark: direct evidence of mitophagy induction in human tissue from a supplement.
  • 2022 (European Journal of Sport Science): RCT in 66 healthy older adults (65–90 years) showed 500mg/day Mitopure® for 4 months significantly improved muscle endurance (hand grip, leg muscle endurance) vs. placebo. No change in muscle strength — suggesting the effect is specifically on mitochondrial function and fatigue resistance rather than hypertrophy.
  • 2023 (Cell Reports Medicine): RCT showed Mitopure® improved mitochondrial gene expression signatures and reduced plasma acylcarnitines (a marker of mitochondrial stress) in both 500mg and 1000mg doses in healthy middle-aged adults.

Other Research Groups

Beyond Amazentis-funded studies, independent research has shown:

  • Urolithin A reduces markers of inflammation (IL-6, TNF-α) in cell and animal models
  • Animal studies show neuroprotective effects — reduced amyloid-beta accumulation in Alzheimer's models and improved cognitive performance
  • Urolithin A extends lifespan in C. elegans, flies, and rodents
  • Anti-cancer properties in cell studies (though no human cancer trial evidence yet)

The Bioavailability Problem (And Why It Matters for Product Choice)

Not all Urolithin A supplements are equivalent. The key issue is bioavailability.

The clinical research that demonstrated muscle endurance benefits used Mitopure® — a proprietary crystalline Urolithin A form developed by Amazentis to achieve consistent, high bioavailability. Crucially, the Amazentis trials showed that natural production of Urolithin A from pomegranate extract is far less reliable than direct Urolithin A supplementation — because gut microbiome composition varies so dramatically between individuals.

This means:

  • Pomegranate extract supplements do NOT reliably deliver Urolithin A — most people convert very little
  • Urolithin A supplements vary in form and purity — cheaper products may use amorphous forms with lower absorption
  • The studied dose is 500mg/day of pure Urolithin A — lower doses are common in cheaper products

How to Take Urolithin A

  • Dose: 500mg/day is the clinically studied dose. Most human trial data used this amount.
  • Timing: Can be taken any time of day. Some people take it with a meal to improve absorption.
  • Timeline: The clinical trials measured outcomes over 4 months. Don't expect acute effects — Urolithin A works by gradually improving mitochondrial quality, which takes weeks to months to manifest as measurable performance improvements.
  • Stacking: Urolithin A pairs well with other mitochondrial support compounds: CoQ10, PQQ, or NMN/NR (which boost NAD+ for mitochondrial energy production). Some longevity-focused supplement users take all of these together.

Who Should Consider Urolithin A?

Based on the current evidence, Urolithin A makes most sense for:

  • Adults over 40 — mitochondrial decline accelerates after 40; this is the target population for most clinical research
  • Active adults experiencing fatigue or endurance decline — the most robustly demonstrated benefit is muscle endurance, not strength
  • Longevity enthusiasts — those building a comprehensive mitochondrial health and anti-aging supplement stack
  • People with poor gut microbiome diversity — those who have taken frequent antibiotics or have digestive issues may not produce Urolithin A naturally at all

Urolithin A is generally well-tolerated in all clinical trials to date. No significant adverse effects have been reported at 500–1000mg doses.

The Bottom Line

Urolithin A stands out in the crowded longevity supplement space because it has genuine human clinical evidence — not just cell culture or animal data. The 2019 Nature Metabolism trial and 2022 muscle endurance RCT are landmark studies that demonstrate a supplement can activate mitophagy in humans and translate that into measurable functional improvements.

The caveat: Mitopure® (the clinically validated form) is expensive. Generic Urolithin A supplements at 500mg/day are a reasonable compromise for most people, with the understanding that bioavailability data on those forms is limited compared to the proprietary crystalline form.

For most adults over 40 who are focused on mitochondrial health and longevity, Urolithin A is one of the more compelling additions to a supplement stack — backed by better human trial data than most of its peers.
