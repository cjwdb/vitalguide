Melatonin Guide: Dosing, Timing, Forms & Best Supplements

By the VitalGuide Editorial Team · April 2026 · 9 min read

Melatonin is one of the most widely used supplements in the United States — and one of the most widely misused. Walk into any pharmacy and you'll find 5mg, 10mg, and even 20mg melatonin tablets presented as standard doses. But decades of sleep research tell a very different story: physiological melatonin levels in humans are measured in picograms per milliliter, and doses as low as 0.1–0.3mg can produce maximum sleep-onset benefit in most adults. The typical 5–10mg supplement dose delivers 10–50x more melatonin than the brain naturally produces — a pharmacological dose, not a physiological one, and one associated with morning grogginess, suppressed natural melatonin production over time, and vivid dreams.

Understanding what melatonin actually does — and what it doesn't do — is the key to using it correctly. It is not a sedative. It does not put you to sleep. It is a chronobiotic: a signal that tells your brain it's dark outside and time to initiate sleep. The distinction matters enormously for how you use it, when you take it, and how much you take. This guide covers everything you need to know.

How Melatonin Works

Melatonin (N-acetyl-5-methoxytryptamine) is produced by the pineal gland in response to darkness. As light levels drop in the evening, the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) — the brain's master circadian clock — signals the pineal gland to begin melatonin secretion. Levels rise over 1–2 hours, peak around 2–4 AM, and fall before dawn. This nightly melatonin surge is the key signal that aligns the body's peripheral clocks (in organs, tissues, and cells throughout the body) with the light-dark cycle.

Melatonin works by binding to MT1 and MT2 receptors in the SCN and other brain areas, where it:

  • Inhibits SCN firing rate — reducing the alerting output of the circadian clock and lowering the neurological "wake signal"
  • Advances or delays circadian phase — taken in the early evening, it advances (shifts earlier) the sleep phase; taken in the morning, it delays it. This phase-shifting property is melatonin's most powerful legitimate application.
  • Lowers core body temperature — working together with other sleep-onset signals to promote the temperature drop associated with sleep initiation
  • Does NOT directly sedate — melatonin has no action on GABA receptors (the target of most sleep drugs) and produces no pharmacological sedation at physiological doses

The Dose Problem: Why Less Is More

The most important practical insight in melatonin research is that effective doses are much lower than what's sold in stores. A landmark dose-response study by Lewy et al. found that 0.5mg of melatonin was as effective at shifting circadian phase as 3mg, and that 0.1–0.3mg produced maximal MT1/MT2 receptor binding in the SCN. Higher doses simply spill over into the bloodstream without providing additional sleep benefit, but they do produce higher plasma concentrations throughout the night — leading to morning grogginess and potentially downregulating the body's own melatonin receptors over time with chronic high-dose use.

The scientific consensus among sleep researchers (including those at the Harvard Division of Sleep Medicine and the American Academy of Sleep Medicine) is that effective melatonin dosing for sleep onset starts at 0.5mg and rarely needs to exceed 1–3mg for healthy adults. The 5–10mg doses commonly sold reflect commercial and regulatory decisions, not clinical evidence.

When to Take Melatonin: Timing Is Everything

  • For sleep onset (insomnia): Take 0.5–1mg approximately 30–60 minutes before your desired bedtime. The goal is to reinforce the natural melatonin rise that should already be occurring at this time.
  • For jet lag (westward travel): Take 0.5–3mg at your destination's local bedtime for 2–3 nights. Westward travel is easier to adjust to, and melatonin helps anchor the new sleep time.
  • For jet lag (eastward travel): Take 0.5–3mg at the destination's local bedtime for 3–5 nights. Eastward jet lag is harder to overcome; morning light exposure at the destination is equally important.
  • For shift workers: Consult a sleep specialist — shift work timing is highly individual and melatonin timing must be calibrated precisely to avoid worsening circadian disruption.
  • Avoid: Taking melatonin at random times during the day, in the morning, or in large doses "just in case" — this can disrupt, not improve, your circadian rhythm.

Immediate-Release vs. Extended-Release Melatonin

Most melatonin supplements are immediate-release (IR): they dissolve quickly, raise blood melatonin levels within 30–60 minutes, and return to baseline within 4–6 hours. This profile is best for sleep onset difficulties — trouble falling asleep at a specific time.

Extended-release (ER) melatonin releases the hormone slowly over 6–8 hours, mimicking the natural nocturnal melatonin curve more closely. ER is better suited for sleep maintenance difficulties — waking up in the middle of the night and struggling to return to sleep. The trade-off: ER takes longer to onset (can be 1–2 hours) and may cause more morning grogginess at higher doses.

Some products combine a small immediate-release layer (for onset) with an extended-release core (for maintenance) — a sensible design for those with both onset and maintenance issues.

Special Populations

  • Older adults (60+): Natural melatonin production declines significantly with age, and older adults are more sensitive to melatonin's sleep-promoting effects. Start at 0.5mg; extended-release formulas are particularly beneficial for age-related sleep fragmentation.
  • Children: Melatonin is widely used for children with ADHD or autism-related sleep difficulties but should only be used under pediatric guidance. Doses of 0.5–1mg are typically used; long-term effects on puberty timing are not fully established.
  • Pregnancy: Melatonin crosses the placenta. Pregnant women should consult their OB before using melatonin beyond occasional use.

The Bottom Line

Melatonin works — but not in the way most people use it. It is a circadian signal, not a sleeping pill, and the optimal dose is far lower than what most products provide. If you've tried melatonin and found it either ineffective or left you groggy the next morning, the most likely explanation is that you were using too much. Start with 0.3–1mg taken 30–60 minutes before bed, ensure the room is dark (blue light blocks melatonin production), and give it a consistent schedule for several nights before evaluating results. Used correctly, low-dose melatonin is one of the safest and most evidence-backed tools available for sleep onset support and jet lag recovery.
