Exogenous Ketone Supplements: BHB Salts, Ketone Esters & What the Science Says (2026)

Your body can run on ketones without going full keto. Here's the honest science behind exogenous ketone supplements — and which forms actually deliver results.

By VitalGuide Editorial Team Updated April 2026 12 min read

What Are Exogenous Ketones?

When your body burns fat for fuel, the liver converts fatty acids into ketone bodies — primarily beta-hydroxybutyrate (BHB), acetoacetate, and acetone. Normally, your brain and muscles access ketones only after days of carbohydrate restriction or prolonged fasting. Exogenous ketones let you skip that adaptation window entirely: you drink or eat them, and within 30–60 minutes, blood ketone levels rise measurably regardless of what you ate for breakfast.

The technology has been available in research settings for decades, but consumer products only became widely available in the early 2020s. Today, two main categories dominate the market: BHB salts (BHB bound to sodium, potassium, calcium, or magnesium) and ketone esters (BHB bound to a ketone precursor via an ester bond). They differ substantially in potency, taste, cost, and evidence base.

BHB Salts: Accessible, Affordable, Mild

BHB salts are the most widely sold form. They're made by chemically bonding BHB to a mineral — usually sodium, potassium, calcium, or some combination. Most commercial "ketone drink" powders and ready-to-drink products are BHB salts.

Typical Blood Ketone Elevation

A standard serving of BHB salts (10–12 g of BHB) raises blood BHB to roughly 0.5–1.0 mmol/L — what some researchers call "mild nutritional ketosis." This is meaningful for some applications but well below the 1.5–3.0 mmol/L considered optimal therapeutic ketosis, and far below the 3–7 mmol/L achieved with ketone esters.

Pros of BHB Salts

  • Affordable: $1–3 per serving vs $20–45 for ketone esters
  • Tolerable taste: Most are flavored and drinkable
  • Electrolyte bonus: Sodium/potassium/magnesium content can help hydration
  • Convenient: Available as powders, canned drinks, capsules

Cons of BHB Salts

  • High sodium: Some products deliver 1,000+ mg sodium per serving
  • Modest ketone elevation: May not achieve therapeutic levels for neurological applications
  • GI distress: High doses can cause loose stools, especially with calcium BHB

Ketone Esters: Potent but Challenging

Ketone esters — technically (R)-3-hydroxybutyl (R)-3-hydroxybutyrate or variants thereof — were first developed under DARPA contracts to sustain soldier cognitive performance under caloric restriction. They achieve blood BHB levels 3–5x higher than BHB salts and do so more rapidly.

The tradeoff is palatability. Ketone esters taste profoundly unpleasant — described variously as nail polish remover, acetone, or rubbing alcohol. They also carry a significant price premium: a single serving of KetoneAid or HVMN Ketone-IQ (the two market leaders as of 2026) costs $25–45.

The Research on Ketone Esters

A pivotal 2016 Oxford study by Kieran Clarke and colleagues found that ketone esters increased endurance performance by 2% in trained cyclists at equivalent effort — primarily by sparing glycogen. Subsequent research from the same group and others has demonstrated:

  • Reduced muscle protein breakdown after resistance training
  • Improved reaction time and working memory acutely (though effect sizes are modest)
  • Blunted cortisol response to exercise-induced stress
  • Appetite suppression via GLP-1 pathway activation (the same mechanism as GLP-1 drugs, though milder)

More recent work published in 2024–2025 has explored ketone esters in the context of Alzheimer's prevention — the brain's capacity to metabolize ketones appears preserved even when glucose metabolism is impaired, making ketones a "metabolic bypass" for neurodegeneration risk. These findings are preliminary but have driven significant investor and consumer interest.

Who Actually Benefits from Exogenous Ketones?

Endurance Athletes

The most evidence exists here. Ketones provide ATP more efficiently per unit of oxygen than glucose, making them theoretically ideal for aerobic efforts. Pre-exercise or mid-race ketone ester use may extend time to exhaustion and reduce perceived effort. BHB salts provide a weaker effect but are more practical for daily training.

Cognitive Workers

Many users report sharper focus, reduced brain fog, and more stable energy on days they use exogenous ketones — particularly in a fasted or low-carb state. The brain consumes ketones readily; elevating them even modestly may increase acetyl-CoA availability for neurotransmitter synthesis. Clinical validation of these effects in healthy populations remains limited, but subjective reports are consistent.

People with Epilepsy or Neurological Conditions

The ketogenic diet has FDA recognition for drug-resistant epilepsy. Exogenous ketones are being studied as an adjunct — allowing partial diet flexibility while maintaining ketotic neuroprotection. This is a medical application; consult your neurologist before using ketones therapeutically.

People Transitioning to Low-Carb Diets

The "keto flu" — the first-week fatigue, brain fog, and irritability that accompanies carbohydrate withdrawal — is partly caused by insufficient ketone availability as the liver ramps up fat oxidation. BHB salts can bridge this gap, smoothing the adaptation window and reducing symptom severity.

Realistic Expectations

Exogenous ketones are not a fat-burning pill. They do not cause your body to burn more stored fat — in fact, when you drink BHB, your body preferentially burns that exogenous BHB rather than mobilizing adipose tissue. Weight loss from ketone supplementation is primarily driven by the appetite-suppressing effect and any resulting caloric reduction, not by a direct fat-oxidation increase.

Think of them as a fuel supplement — an alternative energy substrate with cognitive and performance benefits — rather than a metabolic enhancer.

Dosing and Timing

  • For cognitive performance: 10–15 g BHB salts or 25 ml ketone ester 30 minutes before focused work, ideally in a fasted state or 2+ hours post-meal
  • For endurance exercise: Ketone ester (higher potency) 30–45 minutes pre-workout; BHB salts mid-workout for longer sessions
  • For keto adaptation: BHB salts 1–2x daily for the first 2 weeks of dietary ketosis
  • For appetite control: 10 g BHB salts before breakfast or before a meal you anticipate overeating

Safety Considerations

Exogenous ketones are well-tolerated at recommended doses in healthy adults. Known risks include:

  • GI distress: Start at half dose and titrate up over 1–2 weeks
  • Electrolyte imbalance: Ketosis — dietary or exogenous — increases urinary excretion of sodium and potassium; monitor electrolytes if using daily
  • Interaction with diabetes medications: Ketones lower blood glucose modestly; diabetics on insulin or sulfonylureas should consult their physician
  • Pregnancy: Insufficient safety data; avoid unless medically supervised

The Bottom Line

Exogenous ketones are a legitimate tool with real but specific use cases. They're best for endurance athletes seeking a performance edge, cognitive workers who want cleaner mental fuel, and those adapting to a ketogenic diet. They are not a magic weight-loss supplement and should not be expected to substitute for dietary quality or exercise.

For most people, BHB salts are the practical entry point — affordable, easy to use, and good enough for daily cognitive support. If you're a serious endurance athlete or need the strongest possible effect for neurological applications, ketone esters are worth the investment despite the cost and taste challenges.

Quick Summary

  • Exogenous ketones deliver BHB directly — no dietary restriction required
  • BHB salts: affordable, mild (0.5–1 mmol/L); ketone esters: potent, expensive (2–5 mmol/L)
  • Best evidence for endurance performance and cognitive support
  • Not a fat-burning supplement — a fuel supplement
  • GI tolerance builds over 1–2 weeks; start at half dose
