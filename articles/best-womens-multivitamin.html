Best Women's Multivitamin Over 50: Thorne Women's Multi 50+, Garden of Life & More (2026)

By Sarah Mitchell, MS, RDN · Updated May 2026 · 12 min read

A well-formulated women's multivitamin is not a substitute for a good diet — but it's one of the most practical insurance policies available for nutritional gaps that are both common and consequential for women's health. Iron deficiency remains the most prevalent nutrient deficiency globally, affecting primarily women of reproductive age. Folate inadequacy before and during pregnancy is a leading cause of preventable neural tube defects. Vitamin D insufficiency is epidemic across all demographics and particularly linked to bone health, immune function, and mood regulation in women. These are real, measurable deficiencies with real health consequences — and a good multivitamin addresses them efficiently.

The challenge is that most women's multivitamins on the market are poorly formulated — loaded with inferior nutrient forms, inadequate doses of the nutrients women actually need, and unnecessary additives. Picking the right one requires understanding what to look for. This guide does the work for you: here's what actually matters in a women's multivitamin and the best options on the market in 2026.

What to Look for in a Women's Multivitamin

Key Nutrients for Women

  • Iron: Women of reproductive age (before menopause) typically need 18mg/day of iron — significantly more than men's 8mg/day — due to menstrual losses. Many "general" multivitamins under-dose iron. Post-menopausal women generally do NOT need supplemental iron (excess iron is a pro-oxidant). Age-appropriate dosing matters.
  • Folate (not folic acid): Folate is the natural form; folic acid is the synthetic form used in most supplements. A significant percentage of the population (especially those with MTHFR gene variants) cannot efficiently convert folic acid to the active methylfolate form. Look for "methylfolate," "5-MTHF," or "L-methylfolate" on the label.
  • Vitamin D3: Look for D3 (cholecalciferol), not D2 (ergocalciferol). D3 is 2–3x more effective at raising serum vitamin D levels. Dosing of 1,000–2,000 IU in a multivitamin is appropriate; higher doses may be needed for individuals with confirmed deficiency (get tested before supplementing aggressively).
  • Vitamin K2: K2 (specifically MK-7 form) works synergistically with D3 to direct calcium into bones rather than arteries. A quality multivitamin includes both D3 and K2 together.
  • Magnesium: Most women are magnesium-deficient. Look for chelated forms: magnesium glycinate, malate, or citrate — not magnesium oxide, which has poor bioavailability and causes digestive upset.
  • B12: Methylcobalamin (the active form) is preferable to cyanocobalamin. This is especially important for vegans/vegetarians and women over 50, who often have reduced intrinsic factor production.
  • Biotin: Widely included in women's formulas for hair and nail health. Evidence is mixed for those without deficiency, but it's low-risk and meaningful for those who are deficient.

What to Avoid

  • Megadoses of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) — these accumulate in the body; more is not always better
  • Artificial colors, excessive fillers, or sugar-coated gummies with poor nutrient profiles
  • Calcium carbonate (poorly absorbed) vs. calcium citrate or malate
  • Iron in post-menopausal formulas — post-50 women rarely need supplemental iron
