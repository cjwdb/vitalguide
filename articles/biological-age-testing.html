Biological Age Testing 2026: How Old Is Your Body Really?

By Sarah Mitchell, MS, RDN · May 2026 · 15 min read

You may be 45 chronologically, but your body could be aging like a 38-year-old — or a 54-year-old. Chronological age is simply the number of years since you were born. Biological age is a measure of how fast your body's cells and tissues are actually aging, based on measurable molecular markers. And unlike chronological age, biological age can be influenced by your lifestyle choices — and tracked over time to see if those choices are working.

A generation ago, biological aging assessment required expensive laboratory equipment and access to research institutions. Today, several companies offer at-home test kits that use sophisticated epigenetic and glycomic analysis to generate a biological age estimate from a simple blood or saliva sample. This guide explains how they work, how to choose between them, and what they can and cannot tell you.

What Is Biological Age and Why Does It Matter?

Biological age reflects the cumulative damage and functional decline in your body's cells and systems. It matters because people of the same chronological age can have dramatically different risks of age-related disease — and those differences are captured better by biological age markers than by birth year alone.

Research shows that biological age measured by epigenetic clocks predicts:

  • All-cause mortality risk (people who test biologically older die sooner, controlling for chronological age)
  • Cardiovascular disease risk
  • Cancer risk
  • Cognitive decline trajectory
  • Physical function and resilience

Perhaps more valuably, biological age can be modified. Studies show that exercise, caloric restriction, improved sleep, stress reduction, and targeted supplementation can measurably reduce biological age as measured by epigenetic clocks. This makes biological age testing a useful feedback tool for people actively working to optimize their healthspan.

Types of Biological Age Tests

Epigenetic Clocks (DNA Methylation)

The most scientifically validated biological age tests measure DNA methylation — chemical modifications to DNA that change predictably with age. Steve Horvath's original 2013 epigenetic clock identified 353 CpG methylation sites that together predict biological age with remarkable accuracy. Newer "second-generation" clocks (PhenoAge, GrimAge, DunedinPACE) improve on this by incorporating phenotypic data and predicting not just biological age but pace of aging and disease risk.

Key epigenetic clocks used in commercial tests:

  • DunedinPACE: Measures pace of aging (not just current biological age) — how fast you are aging right now, expressed as a rate relative to the population average of 1.0
  • PhenoAge: Calibrated against clinical biomarkers; good predictor of mortality and morbidity risk
  • GrimAge: The strongest predictor of life expectancy in research literature; based on smoking pack-years and plasma protein markers in addition to methylation
  • Horvath Pan-Tissue Clock: The original, validated across 51 tissue types

Glycan Tests

Glycans are sugar chains attached to proteins in the blood. N-glycan patterns on immunoglobulin G (IgG) change predictably with biological aging and inflammation. GlycanAge measures these patterns to generate a biological age estimate, with particular sensitivity to lifestyle-driven changes — making it especially useful for tracking the impact of interventions like diet changes or exercise programs.

Telomere Length Tests

Telomeres are protective caps on chromosomes that shorten with cell division and stress. Telomere length testing provides a broad aging signal but has higher measurement variability than epigenetic clocks and is considered a less precise biological age indicator by most aging researchers. Still useful as one data point in a comprehensive assessment.

How to Use Biological Age Test Results

What a Single Test Tells You

A single biological age measurement gives you a baseline — but the real value comes from tracking over time. If your biological age is 5 years younger than your chronological age, that's a positive signal. If it's older, it's a motivating data point, not a diagnosis. Single measurements also have measurement variability — a reading 2–3 years in either direction of your true biological age is within expected error margins for most tests.

Lifestyle Factors That Measurably Reduce Biological Age

Research using epigenetic clocks has quantified the biological age impact of specific interventions:

  • Exercise: Regular high-intensity exercise is associated with 4–10 years younger biological age in cross-sectional studies. The biggest effects come from consistent aerobic training and resistance training combined.
  • Caloric restriction and time-restricted eating: A clinical trial (CALERIE study) found that 25% caloric restriction over 2 years reduced DunedinPACE (aging pace) significantly compared to controls.
  • Sleep: Poor sleep is one of the strongest predictors of accelerated epigenetic aging. Getting 7–9 hours of quality sleep is a primary lever for biological age.
  • Diet quality: Mediterranean diet adherence is consistently associated with younger biological age. Processed food consumption accelerates epigenetic aging.
  • Stress management: Chronic psychological stress accelerates DNA methylation-based aging. Mindfulness and stress reduction interventions show measurable epigenetic benefits in RCTs.
  • Smoking: One of the strongest accelerators of biological age — GrimAge (the most mortality-predictive clock) was partly trained on smoking data because of its outsized effect.

Testing Frequency

Annual testing is appropriate for most people using biological age as a general longevity metric. Those actively implementing major lifestyle interventions (e.g., a new diet protocol, training program, or supplement stack) may benefit from 6-month retesting to assess impact. More frequent testing generally isn't meaningful given biological age measurement variability and the pace at which these markers change.

What Biological Age Tests Cannot Tell You

  • They don't predict individual lifespan — they provide population-level risk information, not your specific future health trajectory
  • They don't replace clinical diagnostics — a favorable biological age doesn't mean you can skip cardiovascular screening, colonoscopy, or other preventive medical care
  • Measurement variability: No current test is accurate to within 1 year of true biological age — all have meaningful measurement error
  • Tissue specificity: Blood-based tests reflect blood cell aging; different tissues may age at different rates

The Bottom Line

Biological age testing is one of the most exciting developments in consumer health monitoring — it puts quantitative metrics on aging biology that were previously accessible only to research institutions. For longevity-focused individuals who want the most comprehensive analysis, TruDiagnostic TruAge PACE is the gold standard. For those prioritizing ease of collection and budget, Elysium Index is the best entry point. For people actively trying to reverse-age through lifestyle interventions and wanting rapid, sensitive feedback, GlycanAge provides the best signal-to-intervention timeline.

Pair biological age testing with the interventions shown to actually move the needle: consistent exercise (especially resistance training and Zone 2 cardio), quality sleep, anti-inflammatory nutrition, and stress management. Test, intervene, and retest.
