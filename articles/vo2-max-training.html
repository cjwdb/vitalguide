VO2 Max: The Single Most Important Number for Your Health and Longevity

By the VitalGuide Editorial Team · April 2026 · 11 min read

If you could track only one fitness metric for the rest of your life, make it VO2 max. A landmark 2018 study in JAMA Network Open — tracking over 122,000 patients for more than two decades — found that cardiorespiratory fitness, measured by VO2 max, was the strongest predictor of all-cause mortality ever recorded. Having a low VO2 max carried a higher mortality risk than smoking, hypertension, or diabetes. Being in the top quartile of VO2 max for your age was associated with a 5-fold reduction in mortality risk compared to the bottom quartile.

Physician and longevity researcher Peter Attia has called VO2 max "the most important vital sign we're not measuring." The reason is simple: VO2 max encapsulates your heart, lung, circulatory, and muscular systems' collective capacity to do work — and the decline of these systems is the primary driver of death from cardiovascular disease, the number one killer worldwide.

The good news: VO2 max is highly trainable at any age. With the right protocols, most people can improve their VO2 max significantly within 8–12 weeks — and maintaining a high VO2 max into your 60s and 70s may be the single most powerful thing you can do for longevity.

What Is VO2 Max?

VO2 max (maximal oxygen uptake) is the maximum rate at which your body can consume oxygen during intense exercise, measured in milliliters of oxygen per kilogram of body weight per minute (mL/kg/min). It reflects the integrated efficiency of:

  • Cardiac output: How much blood your heart pumps per minute (stroke volume × heart rate)
  • Oxygen extraction: How efficiently your muscles extract oxygen from the blood (the arteriovenous oxygen difference)
  • Mitochondrial density: How many mitochondria your muscle cells contain and how efficiently they use oxygen to produce ATP

A sedentary adult typically has a VO2 max of 30–40 mL/kg/min. Elite endurance athletes reach 70–85 mL/kg/min. The average 40-year-old loses about 1% of VO2 max per year after age 30 — which means someone who is average at 40 may be clinically impaired by 70 without intervention. Training can offset this decline dramatically.

How to Measure Your VO2 Max

Gold Standard: Lab Test (VO2 Max Test)

The most accurate measurement is a graded exercise test (GXT) on a treadmill or cycle ergometer in a sports science lab, while breathing through a metabolic analyzer that measures oxygen consumption and carbon dioxide production in real time. This gives a precise VO2 max reading. Many university exercise science departments, cardiology centers, and sports performance facilities offer this test for $100–200.

Wearable Estimates

Modern fitness wearables use heart rate variability, resting heart rate, and exercise data to estimate VO2 max with reasonable accuracy. Garmin devices use a proprietary algorithm called FirstBeat that has been validated in multiple studies to within ±3.5 mL/kg/min of lab measurements. Apple Watch, Polar, and Whoop also offer VO2 max estimates. These are not as precise as lab tests but are excellent for tracking trends over time.

Cooper Test (Simple Field Test)

Run as far as possible in 12 minutes. Use the formula: VO2 max = (distance in meters − 504.9) / 44.73. Not as accurate as lab testing but requires nothing beyond a track and a watch — useful for baselines without equipment.

The Two Training Protocols That Work Best

1. Zone 2 Training (The Foundation)

Zone 2 training — moderate-intensity aerobic exercise at roughly 60–70% of maximum heart rate — builds the aerobic base and mitochondrial density that supports a high VO2 max. You should be able to hold a conversation but feel like you're genuinely working. Zone 2 improves fat oxidation efficiency, lactate clearance, and mitochondrial biogenesis in slow-twitch muscle fibers. It's the bread-and-butter of professional endurance athletes' training volume (80% of their total training).

Protocol: 3–4 sessions per week, 45–60 minutes each, at a pace where you can speak in full sentences but not comfortably sing. Heart rate approximately 60–70% of HRmax (roughly: 220 minus your age, multiplied by 0.65).

2. High-Intensity Interval Training (VO2 Max Intervals)

To raise your VO2 max ceiling, you need to train at or near it. High-intensity intervals — done at 90–100% of VO2 max — force cardiac adaptations (increased stroke volume, improved oxygen delivery) and drive mitochondrial biogenesis more rapidly than Zone 2 alone. The most validated protocol is the Norwegian 4×4 method, developed by Dr. Jan Helgerud:

Norwegian 4×4 Protocol:

  • Warm up 10 minutes at moderate intensity
  • 4 intervals of 4 minutes at 90–95% of maximum heart rate
  • 3 minutes of active recovery at ~70% HRmax between each interval
  • Cool down 5 minutes easy
  • Total session: ~35 minutes
  • Frequency: 2× per week

A 2007 study by Helgerud et al. in Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise found the 4×4 protocol produced larger VO2 max gains than all other interval formats tested. A 2019 meta-analysis confirmed HIIT produces VO2 max improvements averaging 5–6 mL/kg/min over 8–12 weeks in previously untrained adults.

VO2 Max by Age: How Do You Stack Up?

The following are approximate age-adjusted VO2 max ranges (mL/kg/min) for men and women:

  • Excellent (top 20%): Men 40–49: >42 | Women 40–49: >35
  • Good: Men 40–49: 36–42 | Women 40–49: 30–35
  • Average: Men 40–49: 30–35 | Women 40–49: 24–29
  • Poor (bottom 20%): Men 40–49: <30 | Women 40–49: <24

Peter Attia's target: being in the top 2.5% for your age and sex in your 40s–50s, which translates to roughly 50+ mL/kg/min for men in midlife. Ambitious — but achievable with consistent training.

Practical Weekly Structure

For most people, combining Zone 2 and VO2 max intervals in a weekly structure produces the best results:

  • Monday: 45–60 min Zone 2 (run, bike, or row)
  • Tuesday: Rest or light activity
  • Wednesday: Norwegian 4×4 intervals
  • Thursday: 45–60 min Zone 2
  • Friday: Rest or strength training
  • Saturday: Norwegian 4×4 intervals
  • Sunday: Long Zone 2 session (60–90 min)

This 5-day aerobic structure is consistent with what elite endurance athletes call "polarized training" — spending most time in low-intensity Zone 2, with 2 high-intensity sessions for maximal aerobic adaptation.

The Bottom Line

VO2 max is the most powerful biomarker of longevity currently measurable without a blood test — and it's trainable at any age. The combination of Zone 2 cardio (building aerobic base) and high-intensity intervals like the Norwegian 4×4 (pushing the VO2 max ceiling) is the evidence-based strategy for maximizing it. Even modest improvements — moving from "poor" to "good" for your age group — are associated with substantial reductions in cardiovascular and all-cause mortality risk. Start tracking it. Start training it. It may be the most important thing you do for your long-term health.
