Best Sleep Supplements 2026: Beyond Melatonin — What Actually Works

By Sarah Mitchell, MS, RDN · April 2026 · 13 min read

If your approach to sleep supplements begins and ends with melatonin, you're working with one piece of a much larger puzzle.

Melatonin is useful — primarily as a circadian rhythm signal for jet lag, shift work, and delayed sleep phase — but it's not a sedative, it doesn't meaningfully improve sleep quality, and it's chronically overdosed by most people who take it. The 10 mg gummies sold in every grocery store contain 10–20x the dose that research supports for its intended purpose.

The good news: several other sleep compounds have strong clinical evidence that address the mechanisms melatonin doesn't touch — anxiety-driven insomnia, temperature regulation, GABA modulation, and sleep architecture.

Understanding Why You Can't Sleep

Most people who struggle with sleep fall into one of three categories:

  • Trouble falling asleep (sleep latency): Racing thoughts, anxiety, excessive screen light before bed. Best supplements: L-theanine, apigenin, low-dose melatonin.
  • Trouble staying asleep (sleep maintenance insomnia): Blood sugar fluctuations, hot sleeping environment, stress hormones, alcohol use. Best supplements: magnesium glycinate, glycine, ashwagandha.
  • Poor sleep quality (light, unrefreshing sleep): Magnesium deficiency, high cortisol, mineral imbalances. Best supplements: magnesium glycinate or threonate, glycine.

The Evidence-Backed Sleep Ingredients

Magnesium Glycinate (200–400 mg)

Magnesium is involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions, including GABA receptor function (the primary inhibitory neurotransmitter that produces calm and drowsiness). Approximately 50–70% of adults consume below the RDA — and deficiency directly impairs sleep quality. The glycinate form is the most bioavailable and GI-friendly for sleep use.

L-Theanine (100–200 mg)

An amino acid found in green tea that promotes alpha brain wave activity — the relaxed-but-alert state ideal for winding down. Research shows L-theanine reduces anxiety symptoms, improves subjective sleep quality, and reduces sleep latency. Does not cause dependence.

Apigenin (50 mg)

A flavonoid found in chamomile that acts as a partial agonist at benzodiazepine binding sites on GABA-A receptors — producing mild anxiolytic and sedative effects through a mechanism similar to prescription anxiety medications, but far weaker and without dependence risk at typical supplement doses.

Glycine (3 g)

Glycine improves sleep quality through a unique mechanism: it lowers core body temperature (CBT) by directing blood flow to extremities. CBT drop is one of the primary physiological signals that triggers sleep initiation. Research shows 3 g glycine before bed meaningfully improves subjective sleep quality, reduces daytime fatigue, and shortens sleep latency — without any morning drowsiness effect.

Melatonin — Used Correctly (0.5–1 mg)

Most people take too much melatonin. The correct dose is 0.5–1 mg, taken 30–60 minutes before your target sleep time. Higher doses don't improve sleep — they extend the melatonin half-life in your bloodstream and cause morning grogginess. Melatonin's strongest evidence is for jet lag, shift work, and circadian rhythm disruption, not chronic insomnia.

What Won't Work (Oversold Sleep Supplements)

  • Valerian root: Inconsistent evidence; multiple null trials. Not worth prioritizing.
  • CBD for sleep: Current evidence is weak and mixed at typical commercial doses.
  • High-dose melatonin (5–10 mg): Pharmacological overdosing. Higher doses cause morning grogginess, not better sleep.
  • ZzzQuil and OTC antihistamines: Cause rapid tolerance (ineffective within days), suppress REM sleep, and have significant anticholinergic effects. Avoid for regular use.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is melatonin safe to take every night?

Short-term use appears safe. Nightly long-term use is more debated — melatonin is a hormone, and chronic supplementation may affect endogenous production. For nightly use, non-hormonal alternatives like magnesium glycinate, L-theanine, and glycine are safer choices for sustained use.

What is the best sleep supplement that is not melatonin?

Magnesium glycinate (200–400 mg elemental), L-theanine (100–200 mg), glycine (3 g), and apigenin (50 mg) are the four best non-melatonin sleep compounds. Combining two or three provides broader coverage across different sleep mechanisms.

How long before bed should I take sleep supplements?

30–60 minutes before your target sleep time for most compounds. Melatonin specifically should be taken relative to your target sleep time on your circadian schedule, not whenever you happen to go to bed.

Can I take L-theanine and melatonin together?

Yes, they're complementary — different mechanisms, commonly combined, and generally safe together. Keep melatonin at 0.5–1 mg when combining to avoid next-day grogginess.
