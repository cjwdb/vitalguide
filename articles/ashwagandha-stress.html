Ashwagandha for Stress & Cortisol: What the Science Actually Shows (2026)

By the VitalGuide Editorial Team · April 2026 · 14 min read

Chronic stress is one of the most damaging forces in modern health. Elevated cortisol — the body's primary stress hormone — disrupts sleep, accelerates fat storage around the midsection, suppresses immune function, impairs memory, and drives inflammation. When people look for a natural solution, ashwagandha (Withania somnifera) consistently rises to the top. And unlike most herbal supplements, ashwagandha has the clinical research to back the hype.

In 2026, ashwagandha is one of the most searched wellness supplements in the world, and for good reason. Multiple well-designed randomized controlled trials have confirmed its ability to reduce cortisol, lower perceived stress, improve sleep quality, and even boost testosterone in men under chronic stress. Here is everything you need to know.

What Is Ashwagandha?

Ashwagandha is a small woody shrub native to India, North Africa, and the Middle East. It has been used for over 3,000 years in Ayurvedic medicine as a rasayana — a rejuvenating herb for promoting vitality and longevity. Its active compounds are primarily withanolides, a class of steroidal lactones found in the root and leaf that appear responsible for most of the documented effects.

In Western medicine terminology, ashwagandha is classified as an adaptogen — a substance that helps the body adapt to physical and psychological stress by modulating the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, the central stress response system.

The Clinical Evidence: What Research Shows

1. Cortisol Reduction

The most replicated finding in ashwagandha research is its ability to measurably reduce serum cortisol. A landmark 2012 double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled trial published in the Indian Journal of Psychological Medicine found that 300mg twice daily of high-concentration ashwagandha root extract (KSM-66) reduced cortisol levels by 27.9% compared to placebo over 60 days — alongside significant reductions in stress scores on the Perceived Stress Scale.

A 2019 trial in Medicine found similar results: 240mg/day of ashwagandha extract reduced cortisol by 22.2% and significantly improved morning cortisol awakening response, a marker of HPA axis function. Notably, stress, anxiety, and food cravings were also significantly reduced.

2. Anxiety and Stress

Multiple trials have used validated anxiety scales (GAD-7, DASS-21, PSS) to measure ashwagandha's effects on anxiety. A 2019 meta-analysis in the Journal of Alternative and Complementary Medicine analyzing five randomized controlled trials found consistent, statistically significant reductions in anxiety and stress scores compared to placebo, with no serious adverse effects reported.

A 2021 trial published in PLOS ONE found 600mg/day KSM-66 reduced anxiety scores by 41% and significantly improved general well-being, quality of life, and sleep quality over 8 weeks.

3. Sleep Quality

Because stress and sleep are deeply intertwined, ashwagandha's cortisol-lowering effects translate directly into improved sleep. A 2020 trial in PLOS ONE specifically studied sleep outcomes and found that 600mg/day KSM-66 significantly improved sleep onset latency, total sleep time, sleep efficiency, and morning alertness compared to placebo in adults with insomnia and anxiety. The sleep quality improvement was both statistically significant and clinically meaningful.

4. Testosterone and Male Vitality

Chronic cortisol elevation suppresses testosterone production — it's a well-established physiological antagonism. A 2015 randomized trial in Fertility and Sterility found ashwagandha supplementation in men with low sperm count increased testosterone by 17%, improved sperm quality, and reduced oxidative stress markers. A 2019 trial in healthy, resistance-trained men found ashwagandha at 600mg/day increased testosterone by 15% over 8 weeks compared to placebo, alongside improvements in muscle strength and recovery.

5. Cognitive Function Under Stress

Stress impairs memory, attention, and executive function through cortisol's neurotoxic effects on the hippocampus. A 2017 randomized trial in Journal of Dietary Supplements found ashwagandha root extract (300mg twice daily) significantly improved immediate and general memory, executive function, processing speed, and sustained attention compared to placebo after 8 weeks in adults with mild cognitive impairment.

Who Benefits Most from Ashwagandha?

  • People under chronic stress — work pressure, caregiving, financial stress, relationship difficulties
  • People with elevated anxiety — ashwagandha's anxiolytic effects are well documented (though it is not a replacement for professional mental health care)
  • Those with sleep difficulties linked to stress — particularly for difficulty falling asleep and frequent nighttime awakening driven by anxious thoughts
  • Men seeking hormonal support — especially those experiencing low energy and reduced libido possibly linked to high cortisol
  • Athletes in high training loads — overtraining elevates cortisol; ashwagandha may support recovery and reduce training-induced cortisol spikes
  • Vegetarians and vegans — plant-based diets may lack some nutrients supporting HPA axis function; ashwagandha is a clean plant-based option

Dosing: How to Take Ashwagandha

The most well-studied form is a high-concentration root extract standardized to ≥5% withanolides, at doses of 300–600mg per day. Key points:

  • Effective range: 300–600mg per day of a standardized root extract (KSM-66 or Sensoril)
  • Timing: Can be taken once daily or split into two doses. Taking it with food reduces the rare digestive upset some users experience. Many people take it in the evening to leverage its calming, sleep-supporting effects.
  • Onset: Effects build over 4–8 weeks; most trials show significant results at 8 weeks. Some users notice effects within 2–3 weeks.
  • Cycling: Long-term continuous use appears safe in trials up to 6 months. Some practitioners recommend periodic breaks (e.g., 8 weeks on, 2 weeks off) as a precaution, though this is not strongly evidenced.

Forms to look for: KSM-66 (root-only extract, highest quality and most studied) and Sensoril (root + leaf, slightly different withanolide profile, also well-researched) are the two premium proprietary extracts with the most clinical backing. Avoid cheap generic ashwagandha powder with no standardization — withanolide content varies wildly between products.

KSM-66 vs. Sensoril: Which Ashwagandha Extract is Best?

Both KSM-66 and Sensoril are proprietary, standardized extracts of Withania somnifera — the ashwagandha plant. Both have substantial clinical research behind them, and both are meaningfully superior to generic, unstandardized ashwagandha root powder. The choice between them depends on your primary goals.

KSM-66

KSM-66, produced by Ixoreal Biomed, is a full-spectrum root extract — meaning it uses only the root of the plant, which is the form used in traditional Ayurvedic practice. It is standardized to a minimum of 5% withanolides and is the most clinically studied ashwagandha extract in existence, with over 22 randomized controlled trials evaluating it across a range of outcomes including stress, anxiety, cortisol, athletic performance, testosterone, thyroid function, and cognitive performance. The standard clinical dose is 300–600mg per day, with most trials using 300mg twice daily. KSM-66 is particularly well-documented for athletic performance enhancement, testosterone support, and general stress and anxiety reduction.

Sensoril

Sensoril, produced by Natreon Inc., uses both the root and leaf of the ashwagandha plant, which allows for a higher concentration of withanolides — typically 8–10% compared to KSM-66's 5%. This higher concentration means effective doses are lower: 125–250mg per day. The clinical literature on Sensoril describes a somewhat different profile from KSM-66: Sensoril tends to have a more distinctly calming and sedating effect, making it particularly well-suited for anxiety reduction, sleep support, and evening use. With 14+ randomized controlled trials, Sensoril has a strong evidence base, though smaller than KSM-66's. It tends to be slightly more expensive per effective dose than KSM-66.

Ashwagandha and Cortisol: Timing for Maximum Effect

When you take ashwagandha matters — but perhaps not as much as many people think. Here is what the evidence and physiology suggest about optimal timing.

Understanding the Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR)

Cortisol follows a predictable daily rhythm. Levels are lowest in the evening and rise steeply during sleep, peaking approximately 30–45 minutes after waking — a pattern called the cortisol awakening response (CAR). In chronically stressed individuals, the CAR can be exaggerated, leading to morning anxiety, high alertness that feels wired rather than refreshed, and dysregulated stress response throughout the day. A 2019 trial found that ashwagandha supplementation significantly normalized the morning CAR pattern in stressed individuals — suggesting morning dosing may be particularly beneficial for people who experience significant morning anxiety or stress spikes.

Evening Dosing for Sleep

Because cortisol suppresses sleep initiation and reduces sleep quality, the cortisol-lowering effects of ashwagandha translate into sleep benefits when taken in the evening. Sensoril extract, with its more pronounced calming profile, is particularly well-suited to evening use. The 2020 PLOS ONE sleep trial used 600mg KSM-66 at bedtime and demonstrated significant improvements in sleep onset latency, total sleep time, and sleep efficiency. If sleep improvement is your primary goal, taking your full dose 30–60 minutes before bed is a reasonable strategy.

Twice-Daily Dosing

Many KSM-66 studies use a split-dose protocol: 300mg in the morning and 300mg in the evening. This approach maintains more consistent ashwagandha levels in the system throughout the day and is what the landmark Chandrasekhar 2012 cortisol trial used (the study showing 27.9% cortisol reduction). Twice-daily dosing is the protocol with the strongest clinical backing for stress and cortisol reduction.

Consistency Matters More Than Timing

Effects are cumulative and build over weeks. Ashwagandha is not an acute anxiolytic like a benzodiazepine — it works by gradually modulating the HPA axis over time. Taking it at slightly different times each day matters far less than taking it consistently every day. Most trials show significant effects at 8 weeks; some participants notice improvements within 2–3 weeks. If you miss a dose occasionally, don't compensate by doubling up — just continue your normal schedule.

Athletes: Pre-Training Timing

For athletes specifically interested in performance and recovery benefits, some trials have supplemented 30–60 minutes before training sessions. The KSMPHYSIQUE2015 study design used pre-exercise dosing. Whether this timing provides additional benefit over general daily dosing is not clearly established, but it aligns with the body's performance-focused physiology.

Ashwagandha and Thyroid Medication: Important Considerations

Ashwagandha's interaction with thyroid function is one of the most clinically important considerations for supplement safety — and one of the least discussed in general wellness coverage. If you have any thyroid condition or take thyroid medication, you need to understand this before starting ashwagandha.

Ashwagandha Increases Thyroid Hormone Levels

Multiple studies have documented that ashwagandha supplementation can increase serum T3 (triiodothyronine) and T4 (thyroxine) levels. A 2018 study in the Journal of Alternative and Complementary Medicine found that 600mg/day ashwagandha root extract over 8 weeks significantly increased T3 and T4 levels in healthy adults compared to placebo. The mechanisms likely involve ashwagandha's effects on the HPA axis and hypothalamic-pituitary-thyroid (HPT) axis, which share regulatory links.

Implications for Hypothyroidism and Levothyroxine Users

For individuals with hypothyroidism taking levothyroxine (Synthroid, Euthyrox), ashwagandha's thyroid-stimulating effects could alter the effective dose of their medication. In some cases this might be beneficial — a potentially welcome thyroid boost — but it creates a risk of unintended dose changes that could push thyroid hormone levels out of therapeutic range. Anyone on levothyroxine who takes ashwagandha should have thyroid levels (TSH, free T3, free T4) monitored more frequently and consult their endocrinologist before starting.

Implications for Hyperthyroidism and Hashimoto's

For people with hyperthyroidism (overactive thyroid), adding ashwagandha's thyroid-stimulating effects could exacerbate symptoms: heart palpitations, anxiety, heat intolerance, and weight loss. Ashwagandha is generally considered contraindicated in hyperthyroidism. For Hashimoto's thyroiditis (an autoimmune condition that causes hypothyroidism), the situation is complicated further by ashwagandha's immune-stimulating properties — Hashimoto's involves an autoimmune attack on thyroid tissue, and immune stimulation could theoretically worsen the condition. Consultation with an endocrinologist is strongly advised before using ashwagandha in the context of any autoimmune thyroid condition.

Other Drug Interactions to Know

  • Immunosuppressant medications: Ashwagandha has documented immune-stimulating properties (it increases natural killer cell activity and immunoglobulin levels). This immune boost is beneficial for most people but could counteract medications designed to suppress the immune system in transplant patients or those with autoimmune diseases treated with immunosuppressants.
  • Sedative medications and benzodiazepines: Ashwagandha has GABAergic activity (it modulates GABA-A receptors) that contributes to its calming effects. Combined with sedative medications, benzodiazepines, or sleep aids, this additive effect could cause excessive sedation. Use caution and inform your prescribing physician.
  • Thyroid hormone medications: As detailed above — monitor thyroid levels and adjust under physician supervision.

Is Ashwagandha Safe? Side Effects to Know

Ashwagandha has a strong safety profile in clinical trials, but there are considerations:

  • Thyroid interaction: Ashwagandha may increase thyroid hormone levels (T3 and T4). People with hyperthyroidism or those on thyroid medication should consult their physician before use.
  • Pregnancy: Traditionally used to induce abortions in Ayurvedic practice at high doses. Avoid during pregnancy.
  • Autoimmune conditions: Ashwagandha may stimulate the immune system. People with autoimmune conditions (lupus, rheumatoid arthritis, Hashimoto's) should consult their physician.
  • Sedative medications: Additive sedative effects possible; discuss with your doctor if taking sleep medication or benzodiazepines.
  • Liver: Rare case reports of liver injury have been published; causality is not established but warrants monitoring in those with liver conditions.

For healthy adults without the above conditions, standard doses of 300–600mg KSM-66 per day appear safe for up to 6 months based on available clinical data.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is ashwagandha used for?

Ashwagandha is primarily used as an adaptogen for stress reduction, cortisol management, anxiety relief, and sleep support. Clinical research also supports its use for boosting testosterone in men under stress, improving athletic performance and recovery, enhancing cognitive function under chronic stress, and supporting thyroid function. It is one of the most clinically studied herbal supplements in existence, with dozens of randomized controlled trials across these applications.

What dose of ashwagandha should I take?

For KSM-66 extract: 300–600mg per day, typically split as 300mg morning and 300mg evening. For Sensoril extract: 125–250mg per day. These are the dose ranges validated in clinical trials. For unstandardized root powder, higher doses (up to 1,950mg/day) are sometimes used, but efficacy is less predictable due to variable withanolide content. Always match your dose to the extract type — KSM-66 and Sensoril are more potent per milligram than raw powder.

How long does it take for ashwagandha to work?

It depends on what you're taking it for. Sleep improvements tend to emerge earliest — some users report better sleep within 1–2 weeks. Stress and cortisol effects typically become noticeable within 2–4 weeks of consistent daily use. The most significant improvements in anxiety scores in clinical trials are usually observed at the 8-week mark. Testosterone and athletic performance benefits typically require 6–8 weeks to fully manifest. Ashwagandha is not an acute drug — it works through gradual HPA axis modulation, and the effects compound over time with consistent use.

Can I take ashwagandha every day?

Yes — daily use is both safe and the recommended approach for most people. Clinical safety studies have demonstrated well-tolerated daily use for up to 90 days, and some studies have extended safety monitoring to 6 months without concerning signals. Some integrative medicine practitioners recommend periodic cycling — typically 8–12 weeks of daily use followed by a 4-week break — as a conservative precaution to prevent potential tolerance or habituation, though this is not strongly supported by clinical evidence. If you are on medications that interact with ashwagandha (particularly thyroid medications or immunosuppressants), consult your physician about an appropriate protocol before starting.

Does ashwagandha cause liver damage?

This is rare but documented. A small number of case reports have linked ashwagandha supplementation to hepatotoxicity (liver injury), typically presenting as jaundice, elevated liver enzymes, fatigue, and abdominal discomfort. In the reported cases, patients generally recovered after discontinuing the supplement. Causality is not definitively established in all reports, and systematic clinical trials have not found liver enzyme elevations at standard doses. The key precautions: use only recommended doses (300–600mg/day of standardized extract), avoid combining with other potentially hepatotoxic substances, and discontinue immediately and seek medical evaluation if you develop jaundice (yellowing of skin or eyes), dark urine, persistent abdominal pain, or unexplained fatigue. People with existing liver conditions should consult a physician before using ashwagandha.

Can women take ashwagandha?

Yes — the clinical evidence for stress, cortisol, anxiety, and sleep benefits applies equally to women. Research has also shown benefits for female sexual function and quality of life under stress. Women who are pregnant should not take ashwagandha — traditional Ayurvedic texts describe its use to promote uterine contractions, and while the evidence is not definitive at standard supplement doses, it is universally recommended to avoid it during pregnancy as a precaution. Women with thyroid conditions (including Hashimoto's or Graves' disease) should consult their physician given ashwagandha's documented effects on thyroid hormone levels.

Is KSM-66 better than generic ashwagandha?

Yes, for predictability and reliability. KSM-66 is standardized to a minimum of 5% withanolides per batch and has been the subject of over 22 randomized controlled clinical trials. Generic ashwagandha root powder has no guaranteed withanolide content — independent testing has found wide variation between products, including some that contain far less active compound than their labels suggest. With KSM-66, you know exactly what you're getting at the dose validated in clinical research. The premium over generic powder is generally modest and worth paying for certainty of effect.

Can I take ashwagandha with other supplements?

Ashwagandha generally combines well with other adaptogens and stress-management supplements. Common and well-tolerated combinations include magnesium glycinate (synergistic for sleep and anxiety), rhodiola rosea (complementary adaptogen with different mechanisms — rhodiola is more stimulating while ashwagandha is more calming), L-theanine (complementary anxiety support), and vitamin D (which also supports mood and stress resilience). Be cautious combining ashwagandha with other sedative herbs (valerian, kava, passionflower) due to potentially excessive sedation, and always account for all supplements when discussing your regimen with your healthcare provider.
