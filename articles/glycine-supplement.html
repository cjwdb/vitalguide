Glycine: The Overlooked Amino Acid for Sleep, Longevity, and Recovery

By the VitalGuide Editorial Team · Last Updated: April 2026 · 10 min read

Glycine is the simplest amino acid — a single carbon, non-essential molecule that the body produces on its own. Yet research increasingly suggests that endogenous production is insufficient for optimal health, particularly as we age. Glycine is required for collagen synthesis, glutathione production, creatine synthesis, bile acid conjugation, and neurotransmission. The emerging GlyNAC longevity protocol has brought renewed research attention to glycine, with a 2023 clinical trial showing it may actually reverse some hallmarks of biological aging. This guide covers the full picture: what the evidence actually shows, how to dose it, and which products are worth buying.

What Is Glycine?

Glycine (2-aminoacetic acid) is the smallest and simplest of the 20 amino acids. Unlike essential amino acids, the body synthesizes glycine from serine, threonine, choline, and hydroxyproline. However, the amount produced endogenously — estimated at 2–3 grams/day — is likely insufficient for all the roles glycine plays, a concept called "glycine shortage" or "glycine gap." The shortfall is estimated at 10+ grams per day when you account for all metabolic demands.

Glycine's functions are broad:

  • Collagen precursor: One-third of all amino acids in collagen are glycine, making it the single most abundant amino acid in connective tissue
  • Glutathione synthesis: Glycine is a direct precursor to glutathione (GSH), the body's master antioxidant — declining with age alongside NAC (N-acetylcysteine)
  • Creatine synthesis: Required alongside arginine and methionine to form creatine in the kidneys
  • Neurotransmission: Acts as an inhibitory neurotransmitter in the spinal cord and brainstem; in the forebrain it acts as a co-agonist at NMDA receptors (excitatory)
  • Bile acid conjugation: Required for proper fat digestion and absorption of fat-soluble vitamins
  • Core body temperature regulation: Glycine lowers core body temperature slightly, a mechanism underlying its sleep benefits

Glycine and Sleep Quality

This is one of glycine's most convincingly demonstrated effects in humans. A series of Japanese studies from 2007–2012 established that 3 g of glycine taken at bedtime improves subjective sleep quality, reduces daytime sleepiness, and improves performance on cognitive tasks the morning after supplementation — without sedation the following day (unlike melatonin or GABA supplements).

  • A 2007 double-blind crossover study (n=11) found that 3 g glycine at bedtime reduced sleep latency (time to fall asleep), improved sleep efficiency (time asleep / time in bed), and reduced next-day fatigue on both subjective and objective measures (wrist actigraphy, EEG sleep staging)
  • A 2012 study (n=19, men with chronic sleep complaints) found that 3 g glycine at bedtime significantly improved polysomnography measures including time in slow-wave sleep, subjective sleep quality on the St. Mary's Hospital Sleep Questionnaire, and performance on a sustained attention task the following morning
  • The proposed mechanism involves glycine lowering core body temperature by dilating peripheral blood vessels in the extremities, facilitating the natural drop in core body temperature that initiates sleep. This is distinct from the sedative mechanisms of sleep aids and explains why glycine doesn't cause next-day grogginess.

The GlyNAC Longevity Protocol

GlyNAC — a combination of glycine and N-acetylcysteine (NAC) — has emerged as one of the most compelling longevity stacks in recent research. The rationale: glutathione (GSH) declines dramatically with aging (~50% reduction between age 20 and 70), and both glycine and cysteine (from NAC) are rate-limiting precursors to GSH synthesis. Restoring both precursors simultaneously may more effectively restore GSH than supplementing either alone.

Key Clinical Research

A landmark 2021 pilot RCT by Kumar et al. at Baylor College of Medicine (n=8 elderly, n=8 young controls) found that 24 weeks of GlyNAC supplementation in elderly adults (average age 71):

  • Restored glutathione levels in red blood cells to those of young adults
  • Reduced markers of oxidative stress by ~70%
  • Improved mitochondrial fuel oxidation
  • Increased muscle strength and gait speed
  • Reduced markers of inflammation, insulin resistance, and endothelial dysfunction
  • Improved cognitive function on standardized tests

A 2023 follow-up RCT (n=45, randomized, placebo-controlled) by the same group confirmed and extended these findings: GlyNAC supplementation for 16 weeks in elderly adults improved multiple hallmarks of aging simultaneously — oxidative stress, inflammation, mitochondrial function, physical function, and cognitive performance — compared to placebo. The researchers concluded that GlyNAC supplementation "has the potential to be a highly effective geroprotective strategy."

GlyNAC Dosing Protocol

The clinical trials used weight-adjusted dosing of approximately:

  • Glycine: ~100 mg/kg/day (approximately 7–8 g/day for most adults)
  • NAC: ~100 mg/kg/day (approximately 7–8 g/day for most adults)

These doses are substantially higher than most glycine supplements provide. For the sleep benefit, 3 g at bedtime is sufficient. For the GlyNAC longevity protocol, higher doses (6–10 g of each) are needed — typically requiring bulk glycine powder combined with a separate NAC supplement.

Glycine for Athletic Recovery

For athletes, glycine is interesting primarily for three reasons:

  • Collagen synthesis: Glycine + vitamin C + collagen peptides pre-exercise (15–60 minutes before training) has been shown in 2016 and 2019 studies to increase collagen synthesis markers in tendons and ligaments by 2x–3x compared to placebo. This protocol — 15 g collagen + 50 mg vitamin C — is now used by sports medicine practitioners for tendon rehabilitation
  • Creatine precursor: While your body synthesizes creatine from glycine, the rate-limiting step is not usually glycine availability for most healthy people. This effect is secondary to direct creatine supplementation
  • Sleep quality: Better sleep = better recovery. For athletes with poor sleep quality, the 3 g bedtime glycine protocol is particularly valuable for recovery

Glycine Safety and Side Effects

Glycine has an excellent safety profile. It is GRAS (generally recognized as safe) for use as a food additive and has been used in clinical research at doses up to 60 g/day without serious adverse effects. Mild GI effects (loose stools, nausea) can occur at very high doses. The most common practical issue is glycine's mildly sweet taste, which some people dislike in water — try it in a protein shake or juice instead.

  • Drug interactions: Glycine may potentiate clozapine (an antipsychotic) — avoid high-dose glycine if taking clozapine without medical supervision
  • Kidney disease: High-protein/amino acid intake generally requires some caution in kidney disease; discuss with your physician
  • Pregnancy: Dietary glycine is safe; supplemental doses in pregnancy are under-studied

How to Take Glycine

  • For sleep (3 g protocol): Take 3 g (3 capsules or ~1 teaspoon of powder) 30–60 minutes before bedtime. Dissolve powder in warm water or herbal tea.
  • For GlyNAC longevity: Follow the research-guided doses (approximately 7–10 g of each, glycine and NAC, daily). Best taken in divided doses with meals to reduce GI discomfort.
  • For collagen synthesis/tendon recovery: 15 g hydrolyzed collagen + 50 mg vitamin C, 30–60 minutes pre-workout. Glycine powder can substitute for collagen here (aim for 5–10 g glycine) if you prefer.

The Bottom Line

Glycine is genuinely underrated. Three grams at bedtime is one of the few interventions with rigorous human sleep data showing improvement without next-day sedation — a meaningful advantage over melatonin, which can cause grogginess. The GlyNAC longevity protocol is emerging from a phase where anecdotal enthusiasm is being supported by actual randomized trials, and the 2023 Baylor RCT is compelling. For athletes, the collagen-glycine-vitamin C pre-workout protocol for tendon health has become standard practice in elite sports medicine. Bulk glycine powder is inexpensive, safe, and versatile — it's one of the best value-per-dollar additions you can make to a longevity or recovery supplement stack.
