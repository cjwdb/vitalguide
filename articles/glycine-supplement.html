Glycine: The Cheap, Underrated Amino Acid That Improves Sleep, Recovery, and Longevity

By the VitalGuide Editorial Team · April 2026 · 10 min read

Glycine is the simplest amino acid — just two carbons, nitrogen, oxygen, and hydrogen. It costs next to nothing as a supplement, tastes mildly sweet, dissolves instantly in water, and has accumulated one of the most impressive and diverse evidence bases of any sports nutrition ingredient. Yet most athletes have never thought to add it to their stack.

The research on glycine spans sleep quality, muscle recovery, collagen synthesis, joint health, blood sugar regulation, and — most compellingly in 2026 — the GlyNAC longevity protocol that has produced some of the most striking age-reversal data in human clinical trials. Three grams of glycine before bed is one of the most evidence-backed, cost-effective sleep interventions available. And at $0.05 per serving for bulk powder, glycine may be the highest-value supplement per dollar that most athletes are ignoring.

What Is Glycine?

Glycine is a non-essential (conditionally essential) amino acid — meaning the body can synthesize it, but demand often outstrips synthesis, especially under stress, intense training, or aging. Glycine is:

  • The most abundant amino acid in collagen (comprising ~33% of collagen's amino acid content)
  • A precursor to glutathione — the body's master antioxidant — along with cysteine and glutamate
  • An inhibitory neurotransmitter in the brainstem and spinal cord, activating glycine receptors that produce calming, sedative effects
  • A key substrate in creatine biosynthesis (glycine + arginine + methionine → creatine)
  • Involved in bile acid conjugation, one-carbon metabolism, and nucleotide synthesis

Modern diets are chronically low in glycine because we predominantly eat muscle meat (high in methionine, low in glycine) rather than bone broth, skin, and connective tissue — which previous generations consumed far more of. This methionine/glycine imbalance has been linked to metabolic dysfunction in animal models.

The Evidence: What Glycine Actually Does

Sleep Quality

The most immediately applicable evidence for glycine is its effect on sleep. A Japanese double-blind RCT (Inagawa et al., 2006) found that 3g of glycine taken 1 hour before bed significantly improved subjective sleep quality, fatigue, and daytime cognitive function in people with insomnia complaints. A follow-up study (Bannai et al., 2012) found that 3g of glycine reduced sleep-onset latency, increased time in slow-wave sleep (SWS), and reduced fatigue the morning after a night of sleep restriction — without any daytime sedation. The mechanism involves glycine lowering core body temperature (via peripheral vasodilation) and acting on NMDA receptors in the brain's suprachiasmatic nucleus to promote sleep onset.

Muscle Recovery and Lean Mass

Glycine plays a less-celebrated but important role in athletic recovery. It is the rate-limiting substrate for collagen synthesis in tendons, ligaments, and cartilage — the connective tissues that are consistently stressed and damaged during training. A 2019 study found glycine supplementation before exercise increased collagen synthesis markers significantly when combined with vitamin C (which is required for collagen crosslinking). Multiple studies have also found glycine reduces muscle protein breakdown during catabolic states (fasting, immobilization) by inhibiting the ubiquitin-proteasome degradation pathway.

Glutathione Production and Antioxidant Defense

Glutathione — the body's primary intracellular antioxidant — is made from three amino acids: glutamate, cysteine, and glycine. As we age, glycine availability becomes the limiting factor for glutathione synthesis, contributing to oxidative stress accumulation. Supplementing glycine has been shown to restore glutathione levels in older adults and in conditions of metabolic stress. This is the foundation of the GlyNAC protocol (see below).

Blood Sugar and Metabolic Health

Glycine enhances insulin-stimulated glucose uptake and has been consistently associated with better insulin sensitivity in epidemiological data. Lower plasma glycine levels are a reliable marker of insulin resistance and type 2 diabetes risk. Multiple intervention studies in people with metabolic syndrome find glycine supplementation improves fasting glucose, insulin resistance, and triglycerides. The mechanism involves glycine's role in gluconeogenesis regulation and its ability to reduce inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6) that impair insulin signaling.

The GlyNAC Protocol: The Longevity Stack Taking the Aging World by Storm

The most exciting recent development in glycine research is GlyNAC — glycine combined with NAC (N-acetyl cysteine). Both are glutathione precursors: glycine provides the glycine substrate, NAC provides the cysteine substrate. Together they restore glutathione synthesis more effectively than either alone.

In a landmark 2021 pilot human trial published in Clinical and Translational Medicine, Dr. Rajagopal Sekhar and colleagues at Baylor College of Medicine gave GlyNAC to older adults for 24 weeks and found remarkable improvements across 13 distinct hallmarks of aging:

  • Glutathione levels restored to near-youthful levels
  • Mitochondrial function significantly improved
  • Oxidative stress reduced
  • Inflammation reduced (multiple cytokines)
  • Insulin resistance improved
  • Endothelial function improved
  • Body composition improved (reduced fat, preserved muscle)
  • Physical strength and exercise capacity improved
  • Cognitive function improved

A 2023 follow-up randomized controlled trial in The Journals of Gerontology confirmed these findings in a larger cohort. GlyNAC is now being studied in multiple academic centers as one of the most promising interventions for reversing multiple hallmarks of aging simultaneously.

Who Should Take Glycine?

  • Athletes and active individuals — for collagen synthesis, connective tissue repair, and improved recovery
  • Anyone with sleep difficulties — 3g before bed is one of the most evidence-backed, zero-side-effect sleep interventions
  • Adults 40+ interested in longevity — the GlyNAC stack has the most compelling human aging reversal data currently available
  • People with insulin resistance or metabolic syndrome — strong evidence for glycine improving glucose and lipid metabolism
  • Those on high-protein (meat) diets — muscle meat is high in methionine but low in glycine; supplemental glycine restores the balance

Dosing

  • Sleep: 3g taken 30–60 minutes before bed; this is the dose used in the published trials
  • Recovery/collagen: 5–10g taken before exercise, ideally with 50mg of vitamin C to support collagen synthesis
  • GlyNAC protocol: 1.33 mmol/kg/day glycine + 0.81 mmol/kg/day NAC; for a 70kg adult this is approximately 8g glycine + 4g NAC daily
  • General health: 3–5g/day; glycine powder has a pleasant mild sweetness and can be added to coffee, tea, or water

Glycine has an exceptional safety profile. It is classified as GRAS (Generally Recognized as Safe) by the FDA. No significant adverse effects have been reported in clinical trials at doses up to 60g/day (extremely high). At typical supplemental doses (3–10g/day), side effects are essentially nonexistent.
